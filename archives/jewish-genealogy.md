# Jewish Genealogy

Jewish genealogy is organized by record type and challenge rather than by country, because Jewish families were dispersed across dozens of nations over centuries. The core difficulties are unique: name changes across multiple languages and transitions, the Holocaust's destruction of entire communities and their records, varying religious and civil record-keeping systems, and the Pale of Settlement confining most Eastern European Jews to specific regions. Despite these challenges, the quantity of surviving material is extraordinary, and dedicated databases have made much of it searchable.

## Key Databases

### JewishGen
- **URL**: https://www.jewishgen.org/
- **Coverage**: The central hub for Jewish genealogy. Databases include the Family Tree of the Jewish People, Communities Database, Yizkor Book translations, vital records indexes, Holocaust databases, and ShtetlSeeker (locating towns by phonetic name matching).
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, location, record type)
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-searchable

### Yad Vashem Central Database of Shoah Victims' Names
- **URL**: https://yvng.yadvashem.org/
- **Coverage**: 4.8 million names of Holocaust victims. Based on Pages of Testimony submitted by survivors and family members, as well as historical documents.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, place, date)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

### ITS / Arolsen Archives
- **URL**: https://arolsen-archives.org/
- **Coverage**: International Tracing Service archives containing 30 million documents: concentration camp records, displaced persons (DP) records, forced labor records, correspondence, and tracing requests. Recently opened for broad online access.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

### USHMM (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum)
- **URL**: https://www.ushmm.org/
- **Coverage**: Holocaust-related records, displaced persons records, ITS/Arolsen Archives access, survivor testimonies, photograph collections.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, collection)
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-searchable

### Avotaynu / Consolidated Jewish Surname Index (CJSI)
- **URL**: https://www.avotaynu.com/
- **Coverage**: Publisher of Jewish genealogy resources. The CJSI is free and searches across 40+ databases simultaneously for surname matches.
- **Cost**: CJSI free; publications and databases are subscription or purchase
- **Searchable**: Yes
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable (CJSI)

### JOWBR (JewishGen Online Worldwide Burial Registry)
- **URL**: https://www.jewishgen.org/databases/cemetery/
- **Coverage**: Jewish cemetery inscriptions from around the world. Includes headstone transcriptions and photographs.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, cemetery, location)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

### Gesher Galicia
- **URL**: https://www.geshergalicia.org/
- **Coverage**: Records from the former province of Galicia (now split between Poland and Ukraine). Cadastral maps, tax records, vital records, and town histories.
- **Cost**: Free (some collections require membership)
- **Searchable**: Yes (some collections indexed)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable for indexed collections

### All Russia Family Tree (VGD)
- **URL**: https://www.vgd.ru/
- **Coverage**: Forum-based research community for Russian Empire records, including Jewish metric books and community records.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Forum search
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-readable (Russian language)

### FamilySearch (Jewish-relevant collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Digitized metric books from the Russian Empire, Austro-Hungarian Empire, and other regions. Growing indexed collections.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **Searchable**: Varies by collection
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for images

## Record Types

### Vital Records (Metric Books)

Jewish communities maintained separate vital records under various state systems:

- **Russian Empire**: Jewish communities kept metric books (metricheskie knigi) under state supervision, recording births, marriages, and deaths in Russian (Cyrillic script). Many are being digitized by FamilySearch. Held in archives across Lithuania, Belarus, Ukraine, Poland, and Moldova.
- **Austro-Hungarian Empire**: Jewish records were kept by the Israelitische Kultusgemeinde (IKG, Jewish community organization). Records are in German or Hungarian. Many survive in national and regional archives.
- **Germany**: Records were kept by local Jewish Gemeinden (communities). Many were destroyed in the Holocaust, but surviving records are in state archives. Some have been microfilmed by FamilySearch.
- **Ottoman Empire / Sephardic world**: Records were kept by local rabbinates. Coverage is uneven. Turkish, Ladino, and Hebrew script.

### Holocaust Records

The single largest category of Jewish genealogical documentation:

- **Pages of Testimony** (Yad Vashem): One-page forms submitted by survivors and relatives documenting individual victims. Include name, birth date, place, family members, and circumstances of death.
- **Transport lists**: Deportation records listing individuals moved to camps. Often include age, address, and transport date.
- **Camp registers**: Intake and administrative records from concentration and labor camps.
- **Displaced persons (DP) records**: Post-war registration of survivors in DP camps. Include pre-war address, family status, and intended destination.
- **Red Cross tracing files**: Correspondence seeking missing family members through the International Committee of the Red Cross.
- **Nuremberg trial documents**: War crimes prosecution records containing witness testimony and organizational documentation.

### Yizkor Books

Memorial books written by survivors documenting destroyed Jewish communities. Over 1,000 published, primarily in Yiddish and Hebrew. They contain names, family relationships, photographs, maps, community histories, and personal narratives that exist in no other source.

Many have been translated (at least partially) into English on JewishGen. The New York Public Library holds a major collection. These are irreplaceable primary sources for reconstructing the history of specific communities (shtetlach).

### Immigration Records

Jewish immigration to the Americas, South Africa, Palestine/Israel, and Australia produced extensive documentation:

- **HIAS (Hebrew Immigrant Aid Society) records**: The primary Jewish immigration assistance organization. Records include arrival information, sponsors, and destination.
- **Ellis Island / Castle Garden passenger lists**: Standard US immigration records, searchable at https://www.libertyellisfoundation.org/
- **Hamburg emigration lists**: Hamburg was the primary departure port for Eastern European Jews. Passenger lists from 1850 to 1934, indexed on Ancestry.com.
- **Bremen records**: Another major departure port, though most Bremen records were destroyed in WWII.
- **Liverpool records**: Transit port for many Eastern European Jewish emigrants heading to North America.

### Landsmanshaftn Records

Immigrant mutual aid societies organized by town of origin. Members who came from the same shtetl formed organizations that maintained membership rolls, cemetery sections, benefit records, and community newsletters. Many deposited their archives with YIVO Institute for Jewish Research in New York (https://www.yivo.org/). These records can identify individuals' towns of origin and connect immigrant families to their pre-migration communities.

### Cemetery Records as Origin Clues

Because those societies owned the cemetery plots, where a person is buried can reveal the family's town of origin:

- **The society / section name points to the town.** Many large urban Jewish cemeteries publish searchable interment databases; the burial record's society or section field often names a hometown society (e.g. "First [Townname]er") that identifies the shtetl.
- **Plot adjacency groups families.** Neighboring graves frequently identify spouses and disambiguate same-name individuals.
- **Caveat:** a married woman was usually buried in her husband's society plot, so it indicates the household's (his) town, not necessarily her own birthplace.
- **Hebrew headstones name the father.** The Hebrew inscription "[name] ben/bat [father's name]" gives the patronymic — recovering a generation for free, often corroborated by a firstborn child named for that ancestor.

## The Pale of Settlement

From 1791 to 1917, most Jews in the Russian Empire were restricted to the Pale of Settlement: present-day Lithuania, Belarus, Ukraine, Moldova, and eastern Poland. Understanding the Pale is essential for Eastern European Jewish research because:

1. **Records are scattered across five modern countries**, each with different archive systems, languages, and access rules.
2. **JewishGen's Communities Database and ShtetlSeeker** help locate the specific town (shtetl) and identify which modern country and archive holds its records.
3. **Town names changed** across Russian, Polish, Lithuanian, Yiddish, and modern spellings. Vilna = Wilno = Vilnius. Kovno = Kowno = Kaunas. Always search using multiple name variants.
4. **Revision lists** (Russian Empire censuses, 1795 to 1858) are a key source for Pale-era families. They list all household members by name, age, and relationship.

## Name Changes

Jewish names changed across multiple transitions, creating one of the most challenging aspects of Jewish genealogy:

- **Yiddish name** (daily use) vs. **Hebrew name** (religious use) vs. **civil name** (government records): A single person might be Moyshe (Yiddish), Moshe (Hebrew), and Moisei (Russian civil records).
- **Russian/Polish/German/Hungarian civil registration** used local-language versions of names. The same person might appear as Moyshe in Yiddish records, Moisei in Russian records, and Moritz in German records.
- **At immigration**: Americanization was common. Moyshe became Morris, Rivka became Rebecca, Chaim became Hyman, Shmuel became Samuel.
- **Surname changes**: Some families changed surnames at immigration or later. Others adopted surnames only when required by law (e.g., the 1787 Austrian decree requiring Jews to take fixed surnames).

**Search tools**: Standard Soundex misses many Jewish name variants. The Daitch-Mokotoff Soundex was designed specifically for Eastern European Jewish names and should always be used alongside standard Soundex. JewishGen's search tools use Daitch-Mokotoff by default.

## DNA and Jewish Genealogy

Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry is one of the most reliably detected genetic populations. Because of centuries of endogamy (marriage within the community), Ashkenazi Jews share more DNA with each other than most populations. This has two practical consequences:

1. **DNA matching finds many relatives**, but the shared DNA segments may reflect more distant relationships than the centimorgan count suggests. Two Ashkenazi Jews sharing 50 cM might be 5th cousins, while two non-endogamous people sharing 50 cM might be 3rd cousins.
2. **Population-level detection is strong**: services like 23andMe and AncestryDNA reliably identify Ashkenazi ancestry even at small percentages.

Tools for Jewish DNA analysis: GEDmatch (free), DNA Painter (for visualizing shared segments), MyHeritage (strong Israeli/Jewish user base), 23andMe (strong Ashkenazi reference panel).

Sephardic and Mizrachi Jewish ancestry is harder to detect genetically because these populations were less endogamous than Ashkenazi Jews.

## Search Strategy

1. **Start with what you know**: family stories, documents, immigration records, naturalization papers. Determine the town of origin (shtetl) as early as possible.
2. **Identify the town**: Use JewishGen's Communities Database and ShtetlSeeker to match family oral history to a specific town and determine which modern country holds its records.
3. **Search JewishGen databases** for that town: vital records indexes, Yizkor Book entries, family trees.
4. **Search Yad Vashem** for Holocaust-era records: Pages of Testimony, transport lists, community documentation.
5. **Search ITS/Arolsen Archives** for camp records, DP records, and tracing files.
6. **Check FamilySearch** for digitized metric books from the relevant archive.
7. **Search JOWBR** for cemetery records in both the origin country and the destination country.
8. **Search YIVO and landsmanshaftn records** for immigrant community documentation.
9. **DNA matching** for connecting to unknown relatives, particularly valuable when paper records are destroyed.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming all records were destroyed in the Holocaust.** Many survived in state archives, particularly in Lithuania, Ukraine, and Poland. Soviet-era archives preserved metric books as government property. JewishGen's databases index many of these surviving records.
- **Conversely, confirm the records actually survive before an exhaustive hunt.** Some district registers genuinely were lost. Resources like Gesher Galicia's district inventories tell you whether a town's pre-war Jewish registers still exist; "town identified, records lost" is a valid, documentable endpoint — not a cue to keep re-searching a dead collection.
- **Not using the Daitch-Mokotoff Soundex.** Standard Soundex was designed for English-language names and misses most Eastern European Jewish name variants. Always search with Daitch-Mokotoff, which is built into JewishGen's search tools.
- **Overlooking Yizkor books.** These community memorial books are irreplaceable. If your ancestor's town has a Yizkor book, it may contain family names, photographs, and community history that survive nowhere else.
- **Not distinguishing between Ashkenazi, Sephardic, and Mizrachi research.** These are fundamentally different research paths: different regions, different languages, different archives, different databases. JewishGen is strongest for Ashkenazi research. Sephardic research involves Iberian, Ottoman, and North African archives. Mizrachi research involves Middle Eastern and Central Asian archives.
- **The Ellis Island name-change myth.** Names were NOT changed at Ellis Island. Immigration inspectors worked from ship manifests prepared at the port of departure. Name changes happened before departure, after arrival, or through gradual Americanization. This myth leads researchers to search for a "real" name that never existed in Ellis Island records.
- **Assuming a single, fixed surname.** Jewish surnames in Eastern Europe were often fluid before the mid-1800s. The same family might appear under different surnames in different records. Patronymics (ben/bat in Hebrew, -ovich/-ovna in Russian) were used alongside or instead of fixed surnames.
- **Ignoring DNA endogamy effects.** Ashkenazi DNA matches require careful analysis because endogamy inflates shared DNA. A match that looks like a 3rd cousin by centimorgan count may actually be a 5th or 6th cousin. Use multiple tools and triangulation.
