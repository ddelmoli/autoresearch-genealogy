# USA: Census Records

Federal census records are the backbone of American genealogy research. Conducted every 10 years since 1790.

## Access

### FamilySearch
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: 1790 to 1950 (all available censuses)
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for individual records

### Ancestry.com
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.com/
- **Coverage**: 1790 to 1950, with the most comprehensive indexing
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only

### NARA (National Archives)
- **URL**: https://www.archives.gov/research/census
- **Coverage**: Original census records
- **Cost**: Free for on-site research
- **AI accessibility**: Some digitized via FamilySearch/Ancestry

## Census Content by Year

| Year | Key Fields Added | Privacy Status |
|---|---|---|
| 1790 to 1840 | Head of household only; others counted by age/sex | Released |
| 1850 | First census to name every person; birthplace (state/country) | Released |
| 1860 | Added personal property value | Released |
| 1870 | Added parents' foreign birth indicator | Released |
| 1880 | Added relationship to head, parents' birthplace | Released |
| 1890 | **Largely destroyed by fire (1921)** | Fragments only |
| 1900 | Added immigration year, years in US, citizenship status, month/year of birth | Released |
| 1910 | Added language spoken, number of children born/surviving | Released |
| 1920 | Added year of naturalization | Released |
| 1930 | Added radio ownership, veteran status | Released |
| 1940 | Added income, education level, place of residence 5 years prior | Released |
| 1950 | Added detailed employment questions | Released (2022) |

## Key Research Notes

### 1890 Census
The 1890 census was almost entirely destroyed in a 1921 fire at the Commerce Department. Only fragments survive (parts of Alabama, DC, Georgia, Illinois, Minnesota, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, Ohio, South Dakota, Texas). This creates a 20-year gap between 1880 and 1900 that complicates research.

### Soundex Indexes
Many censuses have Soundex indexes (coded by sound rather than exact spelling). Soundex encodes a surname as one letter plus three digits. This helps find families even when names are misspelled.

### Enumeration Districts
If you know where your ancestor lived (city and approximate address), you can browse census records by enumeration district even without finding them in the name index. This is useful when the index has errors.

### Census Age Rounding
People frequently rounded their ages on census forms. An age of "30" in 1900 does not necessarily mean birth year 1870. Cross-reference with vital records for exact dates.

### Boarders, Lodgers, and Servants
Non-family members living in a household are listed in the census. These may include recently arrived immigrants staying with relatives, hired help, or elderly parents.

## Search Strategy

1. Identify the census years when your ancestor would have been alive and living in the US
2. Search FamilySearch or Ancestry by name for each relevant census year
3. If not found, try name variants, different spellings, or Soundex code
4. Look at the entire household, not just the target person (spouses, children, parents, and boarders provide additional clues)
5. Note the neighbors: in small towns, the same families appear near each other across multiple censuses
6. Compare census data across decades to track migration, changing occupations, and family composition
