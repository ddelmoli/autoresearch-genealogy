# Canada

Canadian genealogical research is split between federal and provincial jurisdiction. Vital records (births, marriages, deaths) are managed by individual provinces and territories. Immigration, census, military, and citizenship records are federal, held by Library and Archives Canada. Bilingual records (English and French) are common in Quebec and parts of New Brunswick and Ontario. Quebec's Catholic parish registers, beginning in 1621, are among the oldest continuous population records in North America.

## Key Databases

### Library and Archives Canada (LAC)
- **URL**: https://www.bac-lac.gc.ca/
- **Coverage**: Federal records: census (1825 to 1921), immigration and passenger lists (1865 to 1935), military service records, land grants, citizenship records, naturalization indexes.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (catalog and indexed collections)
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-searchable. Catalog and some indexed records are structured; many collections are browse-only images.

### Ancestry.ca
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.ca/
- **Coverage**: Canadian census records, vital records, immigration and passenger lists, military records, and newspapers.
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### FamilySearch (Canada collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Indexed and image collections from all provinces. Includes church records, civil registration indexes, and land records.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for individual records

### Canadiana
- **URL**: https://www.canadiana.ca/
- **Coverage**: Digitized historical publications, city and county directories, and some vital records.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (full-text search of digitized materials)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable for digitized text collections

### AutomatedGenealogy.com
- **URL**: https://automatedgenealogy.com/
- **Coverage**: Indexed 1901, 1906, and 1911 Canadian federal censuses. Volunteer-transcribed.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, province, district)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. Results are structured HTML.

### Quebec PRDH (Programme de recherche en demographie historique)
- **URL**: https://www.prdh-igd.com/
- **Coverage**: Complete reconstruction of Quebec's population from 1621 to 1799, built from Catholic parish registers. The most complete historical demographic database of any population in the world. Includes baptisms, marriages, burials, and family reconstitutions.
- **Cost**: Paid (subscription or per-query)
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, date, parish)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (requires login)

### BMS2000
- **Coverage**: Quebec vital events from Catholic parish registers: baptisms (B), marriages (M), and burials (S, for "sepulture"). Extends coverage beyond the PRDH into the 1800s and 1900s.
- **Cost**: Paid
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, date, parish)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (requires login)

## Census Records

Canadian census history reflects the country's colonial past. Early censuses were colonial or provincial; a standardized federal census began in 1871.

| Year(s) | Coverage | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1666 to 1871 | Various colonial and provincial censuses | Coverage varies by region. New France censuses (1666, 1667, 1681) list heads of household and family members. |
| 1871 | Federal | First post-Confederation census. All provinces. |
| 1881 | Federal | Fully indexed and free on FamilySearch. |
| 1891 | Federal | Available on LAC and Ancestry.ca. |
| 1901 | Federal | Indexed on AutomatedGenealogy.com and Ancestry.ca. |
| 1906 | Federal | Prairie provinces only (Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba). |
| 1911 | Federal | Indexed on AutomatedGenealogy.com and Ancestry.ca. |
| 1921 | Federal | The most recent census available to the public. |
| 1931 onward | Not released | 92-year privacy rule. The 1931 census will be released in 2023. |

The 1881 census is the best starting point for free research; it is comprehensively indexed on FamilySearch and available at no cost.

## Vital Records by Province

Each province manages its own vital records with different start dates and access rules. There is no national vital records database.

### Ontario
- Births (1869+), marriages (1869+), deaths (1869+)
- The Archives of Ontario has older records and indexes online
- Pre-1869: Church records are the primary source

### Quebec
- Catholic parish registers from 1621 (some of the oldest records in North America)
- Civil registration began only in 1994; before that, church records served as civil records
- This unique system means that for centuries, the parish priest's register was the official record of births, marriages, and deaths

### British Columbia
- From 1872
- BC Archives has historical records and indexes available online

### Alberta
- From 1897

### Saskatchewan
- From 1878

### Manitoba
- From 1882

### Maritime Provinces (New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, Prince Edward Island)
- Vary by province, some from the 1860s
- Church records supplement civil registration and often predate it

## Quebec Parish Records

Quebec's Catholic parish registers deserve special attention. They begin with the founding of New France in 1621 and provide nearly continuous coverage of the French-Canadian population for over 400 years.

The registers include baptisms, marriages, and burials (BMS) with exceptional detail. Marriage records are particularly rich: they typically name both sets of parents, witnesses (often relatives), and the parishes of origin. Because French-Canadian families remained overwhelmingly Catholic, these church records cover the vast majority of the population.

The PRDH has used these registers to reconstruct the entire French-Canadian population from 1621 to 1799, linking individuals across generations. This means that most French-Canadian families can be traced back to the original colonists of New France. No other population of comparable size has such complete demographic reconstruction.

For research from 1800 onward, BMS2000 and FamilySearch provide access to parish registers. Many original registers are held by the Bibliotheque et Archives nationales du Quebec (BAnQ).

## Immigration Records

### Pre-1865
Scattered records survive. Passenger lists for Quebec City and Halifax are the primary sources. Many early immigrants, particularly in the French colonial period, arrived through Quebec City.

### 1865 to 1935
LAC holds extensive passenger list images and indexes for arrivals at Canadian ports. These are searchable online and are the backbone of Canadian immigration research.

### Border Crossings from the United States
Recorded from 1908 onward. Soundex indexes and original manifests are available through LAC and Ancestry.ca. These records capture the significant two-way migration between Canada and the US.

### Home Children
Over 100,000 British children were sent to Canada between 1869 and 1948 through various child emigration schemes. They were placed with families, often as agricultural laborers. LAC holds records including shipping lists, inspection reports, and some placement records. The British Isles Family History Society of Greater Ottawa maintains a Home Children database.

## Naming Conventions

### English Canadian
Standard English naming conventions. Fixed hereditary surnames.

### French Canadian
French-Canadian naming has a distinctive feature: the "dit" name system. A "dit" name (also called a "war name" or secondary surname) was used to distinguish branches of the same family. A family might be recorded as "Dubois dit Lafrance," meaning the Dubois family known as "Lafrance." Either name, or both, may appear in records.

In some cases, descendants dropped the original surname entirely and adopted the "dit" name as their primary surname. A researcher looking for the family "Lafrance" might need to search under "Dubois" in earlier records, or vice versa.

French Canadians also commonly used compound first names: Jean-Baptiste, Marie-Anne, Joseph-Arthur. In records, the person might be referred to by either part of the compound name. Jean-Baptiste Tremblay might appear as "Jean" or "Baptiste" in different records.

### Indigenous
Naming conventions vary by nation. Some records use English or French baptismal names alongside indigenous names. Records may contain only the European name, making identification difficult without additional context.

## Metis and Indigenous Records

Metis (mixed French and Indigenous) and First Nations genealogical research draws on specialized record sets:

- **Treaty annuity pay lists**: Annual payments to First Nations under numbered treaties. Lists names, band, and family relationships.
- **Scrip records**: Land or money entitlement records for Metis individuals, particularly in Manitoba and the Northwest. Applications include detailed family information.
- **Hudson's Bay Company records**: Extensive fur trade records held at the Archives of Manitoba. Include employee records, post journals, and correspondence naming Indigenous and Metis individuals.
- **Residential school records**: Sensitive materials managed by the National Centre for Truth and Reconciliation (NCTR) at the University of Manitoba. Access protocols exist for survivors and descendants.

These records are often fragmented across multiple archives and require knowledge of band affiliations, treaty areas, or fur trade post locations.

## Search Strategy

1. Start with the most recent available census (1921) and work backward through earlier censuses to establish the family's location and composition over time
2. Search provincial vital records for the province identified in census records
3. For Quebec families, use the PRDH or FamilySearch church records to trace the family back through parish registers
4. For immigration research, search LAC passenger lists (1865 to 1935) and border crossing records (1908+)
5. For military ancestors, search LAC service files (WWI files are digitized; WWII files require a request)
6. For Metis and First Nations research, search scrip records and treaty annuity pay lists at LAC
7. Cross-reference findings across FamilySearch, Ancestry.ca, and AutomatedGenealogy.com

## Common Pitfalls

- **Not realizing vital records are provincial.** There is no national vital records database for Canada. Each province has its own registry with different start dates, access rules, and digitization levels. You must search the correct province.
- **Quebec's unique system.** Before 1994, Quebec had no civil registration in the modern sense. Church records (almost exclusively Catholic) served as the official record of births, marriages, and deaths. If you search for "civil records" in Quebec before 1994, you will find nothing; search parish registers instead.
- **The "dit" name system confusing non-French researchers.** A family recorded as "Roy dit Desjardins" might appear under Roy, Desjardins, or both in different records and different generations. Failing to search both names will cause missed connections.
- **Privacy restrictions on recent records.** Access restrictions vary by province and record type. Some provinces restrict birth records for 100 years or more. The most recent available federal census is 1921 (the 92-year rule).
- **Assuming US databases cover Canadian records.** American genealogy platforms often have limited Canadian coverage. Ancestry.ca has more Canadian content than Ancestry.com. FamilySearch has good Canadian collections, but LAC and provincial archives remain essential.
- **Ignoring the 1881 census.** The 1881 federal census is fully indexed and free on FamilySearch. It is the single best free starting point for Canadian genealogy and is often overlooked in favor of subscription services.
- **Not accounting for French and English bilingual records.** In Quebec and bilingual regions, records may be in French, English, or both. Names may be translated between languages: Pierre in one record might be Peter in another. Places may also appear under French or English names.
