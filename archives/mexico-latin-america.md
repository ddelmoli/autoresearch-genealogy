# Mexico and Latin America

Latin American genealogy benefits from an enormous advantage: FamilySearch has the largest collection of Latin American genealogy records in the world, built over decades of microfilming by LDS Church volunteers. Catholic church records in Latin America often go back to the 1500s, and many have been indexed. The combination of deep church records, colonial-era archives, and growing civil registration collections makes Latin American research surprisingly productive once you identify the right parish.

## Key Databases

### FamilySearch
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: The dominant platform for Latin American genealogy. Massive collections for Mexico, Brazil, Guatemala, Peru, Chile, Argentina, Colombia, and others. Includes Catholic parish records, civil registration, census records, and immigration records. Many indexed, many image-only.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **Searchable**: Varies by collection (some fully indexed and name-searchable, many browse-only)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for records

### Ancestry.com
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.com/
- **Coverage**: Growing Latin American collections including Mexican civil registration, church records, and immigration. Some indexed collections.
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### AncestryDNA
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.com/dna/
- **Coverage**: DNA matching database with growing Latin American representation. Useful for connecting with relatives across the Americas.
- **Cost**: One-time purchase + subscription for full features
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only

### Archivo General de la Nacion (Mexico)
- **URL**: https://www.gob.mx/agn
- **Coverage**: Mexico's national archives. Colonial records, Inquisition records, land records, census records, and government administration.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Partially (catalog searchable, some digitized collections)
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-readable

### Archivo General de Indias (Spain)
- **URL**: https://pares.mcu.es/
- **Coverage**: Colonial-era records for all of Spanish America. Passenger lists, emigration permits, colonial administration, correspondence, and land grants. Searchable through the PARES portal.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (keyword search and browse)
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-searchable (Spanish language)

### Registro Civil (varies by country)
- **Coverage**: Civil registration offices at the state, province, or municipal level. Access rules vary widely by country and jurisdiction.
- **Cost**: Varies (often small fees for certified copies)
- **Searchable**: Generally not online; requires in-person or mail requests
- **AI accessibility**: Not AI-accessible

## Catholic Church Records (Registros Parroquiales)

The backbone of Latin American genealogy. Catholic parishes recorded baptisms (bautismos), marriages (matrimonios), and burials (entierros) from the earliest colonial period. Coverage begins as early as the 1530s in central Mexico.

These records include:
- **Full names** of the individual and parents
- **Godparents** (padrinos/madrinas), who were often relatives or community leaders
- **Racial/ethnic category** (casta designations in colonial-era records)
- **Legitimacy status** (hijo legitimo, hijo natural, exposito)
- **Parish and town** of residence

Church records are the primary genealogical source for most of Latin America before civil registration. FamilySearch holds the vast majority of microfilmed parish records, and many are now digitized and indexed online.

## Civil Registration

Civil registration began at different dates by country, and compliance was uneven in the early years:

| Country | Start Date | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Mexico | 1859 (law), ~1870s (reliable) | Varied widely by state. Some states began earlier. |
| Brazil | 1889 (Republic era) | Church records continued as primary source in many areas well into the 1900s. |
| Argentina | 1886 (national) | Some provinces began earlier (Buenos Aires 1884). |
| Chile | 1885 | Relatively complete from the start. |
| Colombia | 1860s (in theory) | Uneven compliance. Church records remain important. |
| Peru | 1886 | Compliance improved gradually. |
| Guatemala | 1877 | Relatively complete. |
| Cuba | 1885 | Spanish civil registration from 1885; church records much earlier. |

In all cases, Catholic parish records predate civil registration by centuries and remain the primary source for colonial-era research.

## The Casta System

Colonial-era Latin American records include racial/ethnic categories assigned by Spanish colonial authorities. Common designations include espanol, indio, mestizo, mulato, negro, castizo, morisco, and many others. These "casta" classifications were bureaucratic categories that:

- Changed over time and across regions
- Could be negotiated or purchased (a family classified as mestizo in one generation might appear as espanol in the next)
- Reflected social status as much as ancestry
- Were applied inconsistently by different priests and officials

These designations provide information about how colonial society categorized a family, but they should be interpreted carefully rather than taken as precise statements of ancestry.

## Indigenous Records

Pre-colonial indigenous genealogies exist in some cultures:
- **Aztec/Nahua**: Codices and painted genealogies documenting royal and noble lineages
- **Maya**: Hieroglyphic records and colonial-era community documents (the Popol Vuh, Books of Chilam Balam)
- **Inca**: Quipus (knotted cords) for record keeping; Spanish colonial chroniclers documented Inca royal genealogies

Colonial-era records of indigenous communities include:
- **Tribute lists (padrones)**: Census-like enumerations of tribute-paying indigenous households
- **Church records**: Indigenous parishioners recorded alongside other community members
- **Land records**: Community land grants (mercedes) and disputes
- **Congregacion records**: Documentation of the forced resettlement of indigenous communities into planned towns

Many indigenous communities maintained oral genealogies that parallel or supplement written records.

## Naming Conventions

### Spanish Naming
Two surnames: father's first surname + mother's first surname. Juan Garcia Lopez means Juan, father's surname Garcia, mother's surname Lopez. Same system as in Spain. Women did not change their surnames at marriage.

### Portuguese Naming (Brazil)
Multiple surnames possible; the order is typically mother's surname then father's surname (opposite of Spanish convention). "Da," "dos," "de" are common particles.

### Indigenous Naming
Varies by culture and period. In colonial records, indigenous people may appear with a Spanish or Portuguese baptismal name followed by an indigenous name. In some regions, indigenous surnames were hispanicized (e.g., Nahuatl "Cuauhtli" became "Aguilar").

### "De" and "Del"
These particles can indicate several things depending on context: family connection, geographic origin, or (in colonial records involving enslaved persons) former ownership. "Maria de la Cruz" likely indicates a religious or geographic reference; "Juan del Valle" likely indicates geographic origin.

## Mexican-Specific Sources

### Padrones (Parish Census Records)
Parish-level counts of inhabitants conducted by parish priests, from the 1600s onward. These list heads of household, family members, and sometimes ages, occupations, and casta designations. Particularly valuable for the colonial period.

### Registro Civil (State-Level)
Mexican civil registration is organized at the state level. Each state began at a different date, and record formats vary. FamilySearch has digitized civil registration from many Mexican states.

### Mexican Revolution Records
Military service records, pension records, and government documents from the 1910 to 1920 Revolution. Held at the Archivo General de la Nacion and the Secretaria de la Defensa Nacional.

### Bracero Program Records
Mexican guest workers in the United States from 1942 to 1964. Records are held at the US National Archives (NARA) and include photographs, contracts, and personal information. Searchable at https://www.archives.gov/

## Brazilian-Specific Sources

### Immigration Records
Brazil received massive immigration from Italy, Portugal, Germany, Japan, and other countries in the late 1800s and early 1900s. Ship manifests are held at the Arquivo Nacional and the Memorial do Imigrante (Sao Paulo). Many are digitized on FamilySearch.

### Slavery Records
Brazil was the last country in the Americas to abolish slavery (1888). Records include:
- **Slave trade records**: Ship manifests and customs records documenting the transatlantic trade
- **Manumission letters (cartas de alforria)**: Documents granting freedom, which include the enslaved person's name, owner, and sometimes parents or birthplace
- **Slave registration (matricula)**: The 1872 national slave registration enumerated all enslaved persons by name, age, color, occupation, and owner
- **Baptism records**: Enslaved persons were baptized and recorded in parish registers, often with their owner's name

### Portuguese Colonial Records
For the colonial period, Portuguese parish records at Torre do Tombo (https://digitarq.arquivos.pt/) contain births, marriages, and deaths recorded in Brazil before independence (1822).

## Search Strategy

1. **Determine the state/province and municipality** from family knowledge, immigration records, or naturalization papers
2. **Search FamilySearch** for Catholic church records from that parish. FamilySearch has by far the most Latin American records of any platform.
3. **For post-civil-registration records**, search FamilySearch or contact the local Registro Civil directly
4. **For colonial-era research**, check the Archivo General de Indias via PARES for Spanish colonial records
5. **For immigration**, check records in both the origin country and the destination country
6. **For Mexican research**, check the Archivo General de la Nacion for national-level records
7. **For Brazilian research**, check the Arquivo Nacional and Memorial do Imigrante for immigration records

## Common Pitfalls

- **Not using FamilySearch.** It has by far the most Latin American genealogy records of any platform, and it is free. Many researchers start with Ancestry or DNA testing when the answer is sitting in a FamilySearch parish register.
- **Assuming civil registration records exist before they were mandated.** In many Latin American countries, reliable civil registration did not begin until the 1870s or later. Before that date, Catholic parish records are the only source.
- **Treating casta designations as fixed ethnic identities.** Colonial casta categories were bureaucratic classifications that shifted over time. A family classified as "mestizo" in one generation might appear as "espanol" in the next.
- **Spanish/Portuguese surname conventions confusing English-speaking researchers.** The two-surname system means that a child's "last name" is not the same as the mother's or the father's full surname. Maria Garcia Lopez's children will have the surname Garcia (from their mother's paternal surname) as their second surname, not Lopez.
- **Church records may be in multiple languages.** Depending on the period and region, records may be in Spanish, Portuguese, Latin, or indigenous languages. Colonial-era records from the 1500s and 1600s are often in Latin.
- **Many countries restrict access to recent records.** Several Latin American countries limit access to civil registration records younger than 75 to 100 years. For recent generations, you may need to request records directly from the Registro Civil.
- **Overlooking godparent (padrino) connections.** In Latin American culture, godparents (compadres) were often relatives. Tracking godparent relationships across multiple baptism records can reveal family connections not stated elsewhere.
- **Ignoring the Archivo General de Indias.** For any family with colonial-era roots in Spanish America, the Archivo General de Indias in Seville may hold emigration records, land grants, or administrative correspondence. These records are searchable through PARES and are free.
