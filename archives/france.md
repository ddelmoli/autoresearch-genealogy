# France

France has one of the best-digitized genealogy systems in the world. Each departement has its own archives departementales, and nearly all have digitized their holdings for free online access. Civil registration began in 1792 (French Revolution), making France one of the earliest countries with comprehensive civil records. Parish registers extend back to the 1500s in many areas. The depth and accessibility of French records is exceptional.

## Key Databases

### Archives departementales
- **Coverage**: Each of France's 101 departements has its own website with digitized records. Most have civil registration (from 1792) and parish registers (from the 1500s) available for free browsing.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Browse-only (navigate by commune, record type, year). Each departement has a different interface.
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only. French language, image-based. No single portal; you must visit each departement's site individually.
- **Note**: The main challenge is that each of the 101 departement sites has its own design, navigation, and URL structure. There is no unified national portal for these records.

### Geneanet
- **URL**: https://www.geneanet.org/
- **Coverage**: The largest French genealogy platform. User-contributed family trees, indexed records, collaborative research tools. Over 9 billion entries.
- **Cost**: Free (basic) / Premium subscription for advanced features
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, date, location)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

### Filae
- **URL**: https://www.filae.com/
- **Coverage**: Indexed French civil registration, census records, military records. Strong OCR-based search across digitized records.
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **Searchable**: Yes (indexed, searchable by name)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### FamilySearch (France collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Significant French holdings including indexed and browse-only image collections. Civil registration, parish registers, notarial records.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **Searchable**: Varies by collection
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for records

### Memoire des Hommes
- **URL**: https://www.memoiredeshommes.sga.defense.gouv.fr/
- **Coverage**: French military records including WWI and WWII casualty databases, Indochina, Algeria, and other conflicts. Indexed by name.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, unit, conflict)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

### Archives nationales
- **URL**: https://www.archives-nationales.culture.gouv.fr/
- **Coverage**: National-level records including notarial archives, emigration records, nobility documentation, and records of national significance.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Partially (catalog searchable, records are image-based)
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-readable

### Base Leonore
- **URL**: https://www.leonore.archives-nationales.culture.gouv.fr/
- **Coverage**: Database of Legion of Honor recipients. Dossiers include biographical details, service records, and sometimes photographs.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

## Record Types

### Civil Registration (Etat Civil)

Established in 1792 during the French Revolution. Three record types:

- **Actes de naissance** (birth records): Name, date, parents, witnesses
- **Actes de mariage** (marriage records): Exceptionally detailed. Include both parties' parents (names, ages, occupations, whether alive or deceased), witnesses (often relatives with their relationship stated), and sometimes the parents' formal consent. French marriage records are among the most informative civil documents in European genealogy.
- **Actes de deces** (death records): Name, age, parents, sometimes spouse and occupation

### Tables decennales (Ten-Year Indexes)

Every commune compiled alphabetical indexes of births, marriages, and deaths in ten-year blocks. These are the fastest way to locate a specific record: find the name in the ten-year table, note the record number, then go directly to that entry in the register. Always start with the tables decennales rather than browsing year by year.

### Parish Registers (Registres Paroissiaux)

Before 1792, Catholic parish records are the primary source. Many parishes have records from the 1500s. A critical feature of French parish records: they were kept in duplicate. One copy stayed at the parish, and one copy went to the greffe (court). The court copies often survive even when the parish copy is lost, and vice versa. This redundancy means French pre-revolutionary records have a higher survival rate than most European countries.

Records are in French (the Ordonnance de Villers-Cotterets of 1539 required registers in French rather than Latin), though some early records and records from border regions may be in Latin.

### Conscription Records (Registres Matricules)

France had mandatory military service. Conscription records from 1867 onward contain:
- Physical description (height, hair color, eye color, distinguishing marks)
- Education level
- Occupation
- Complete service history
- Sometimes a photograph (later records)

Many departement archives have digitized these records. They are a uniquely French source with no direct equivalent in most other countries. The physical descriptions are especially valuable for confirming identity.

### Notarial Records (Minutes Notariales)

French notaires recorded contracts, property transactions, marriage contracts, wills, inventories after death, and apprenticeship agreements. These records are extraordinarily detailed and extend back centuries. They are held at departement archives and the Archives nationales.

## The Departement System

France is organized into 101 departements (similar to US counties but with more administrative autonomy). Records are held at the departement level. You need to know which departement your ancestor came from.

Each departement is identified by a two-digit number (e.g., 75 = Paris, 13 = Bouches-du-Rhone, 59 = Nord). These numbers appear on license plates, postal codes, and in archive catalogs. Knowing the departement number helps locate the correct archives website.

To find the departement for a given commune, use the French postal code system or search Geneanet.

## Naming Conventions

French surnames were fixed relatively early. The Ordonnance de Villers-Cotterets (1539) standardized civil record keeping in French.

### Common Patterns
- **de/du** ("of/from"): Can indicate nobility or geographic origin (Dupont = of the bridge)
- **Le/La** ("the"): Descriptive (Legrand = the tall one, Leblanc = the white/fair one)
- **-eau/-aud**: Diminutive suffix (Moreau = little Moore/dark one)
- **dit/dite** ("called"): A secondary name used to distinguish branches of a family. Jean Duval dit Lafleur means "Jean Duval, called Lafleur." The dit name sometimes replaced the original surname over generations.

### Regional Variations
- Alsace and Lorraine: Germanic naming patterns (surnames like Schmitt, Meyer, Hoffmann)
- Brittany: Celtic naming patterns (Le Goff, Kerouac)
- Basque Country: Basque names (Etcheberry, Larreguy)
- Corsica: Italian naming patterns (Bonaparte, Ferracci)

## Search Strategy

1. **Determine the commune and departement** from US immigration records, naturalization papers, or family knowledge. French emigrants often recorded their specific commune of origin.
2. **Search the departement's archives website** for etat civil records. Each of the 101 departements has its own site. A directory of all departement archives is maintained at https://www.francearchives.gouv.fr/
3. **Use the tables decennales** (ten-year indexes) to locate specific records efficiently rather than browsing year by year.
4. **Search Geneanet** for user-contributed family trees that may already connect to your ancestor.
5. **Search Filae** (subscription) for indexed records if the departement archives are difficult to navigate.
6. **For pre-1792 records**, search parish registers at the departement archives. Remember that duplicate copies exist (parish and court).
7. **Check conscription records** (registres matricules) for males born after approximately 1847.
8. **Search Memoire des Hommes** for military service and casualty records.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Each departement website has a different interface.** There is no single national portal. Expect to learn a new navigation system for each departement you research. Bookmark working URLs; some sites reorganize periodically.
- **Not using the tables decennales.** Browsing through years of records page by page is extremely slow. The ten-year alphabetical indexes exist for every commune and are the correct starting point for finding a specific individual.
- **Alsace and Lorraine records may be in German.** These regions were part of Germany from 1871 to 1918. Records from this period are in German, use German naming conventions, and may be in German archives rather than French ones.
- **Confusing communes with the same name.** Like Italian comuni, many French communes share names (Saint-Martin alone appears over 200 times). Always confirm the departement.
- **Overlooking notarial records.** Marriage contracts (contrats de mariage) and post-death inventories (inventaires apres deces) contain extraordinary detail about family relationships, property, and social status. They are underused by researchers who focus only on etat civil.
- **Pre-revolutionary calendar.** During the French Revolution, France used the Republican Calendar (1793 to 1805). Records from this period use unfamiliar month names (Vendemiaire, Brumaire, Frimaire, etc.) and a different year numbering system. Conversion tables are widely available online.
- **Privacy restrictions.** French civil records have privacy periods: birth records are restricted for 75 years, marriage records for 75 years, and death records are generally open. Very recent records may not be available online.
