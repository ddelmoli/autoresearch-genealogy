# Hungary

Hungarian genealogical research is complicated by one central fact: the Kingdom of Hungary (pre-1920) covered territory now in Slovakia, Romania, Serbia, Croatia, Ukraine, and Austria. The 1920 Treaty of Trianon reduced Hungary to roughly a third of its former size. Records from "historic Hungary" are now scattered across multiple countries' archives. If your ancestor was "from Hungary," you must first determine whether their town is in modern Hungary or in one of the successor states.

## Key Databases

### FamilySearch (Hungary collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Large collection of indexed and image-based records. Church records (Catholic, Reformed, Lutheran, Jewish, Greek Catholic), civil registration (post-1895), and census-like records.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **Searchable**: Varies by collection (some indexed, many browse-only)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for images

### Matricula Online
- **URL**: https://data.matricula-online.eu/
- **Coverage**: Catholic parish records from some Hungarian dioceses. Baptisms, marriages, deaths. Browse by diocese and parish.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Browse-only (navigate by diocese, parish, record type, date range)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only. Navigation requires knowing the specific parish.

### Arcanum
- **URL**: https://www.arcanum.com/
- **Coverage**: The largest Hungarian digital content provider. Digitized newspapers, historical documents, some genealogical records. Includes county histories, biographical dictionaries, and periodicals.
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **Searchable**: Full-text search for digitized content
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### Magyar Nemzeti Levéltár (Hungarian National Archives)
- **URL**: https://mnl.gov.hu/
- **Coverage**: Civil registration records (post-1895), county records, noble family records, censuses, conscriptions. Physical archives in Budapest and county seats (megyei levéltárak).
- **Cost**: Free for on-site research; some online finding aids
- **AI accessibility**: Mostly in-person only. Some finding aids are online.

### Radix
- **URL**: https://www.radixindex.com/
- **Coverage**: Indexed Hungarian vital records (baptisms, marriages, deaths) from selected parishes and civil registration offices.
- **Cost**: Free/Freemium
- **Searchable**: Yes (by surname, location, date range)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

## Record Types

### Civil Registration (Anyakönyvezés)

Civil registration began on **October 1, 1895** in Hungary. Before that date, all vital records were maintained by churches. Civil registration records include:

- **Születési anyakönyv** (birth register): Name, date of birth, parents' names, occupations, religion
- **Házassági anyakönyv** (marriage register): Names, ages, occupations, parents, witnesses, religion
- **Halálozási anyakönyv** (death register): Name, age, occupation, cause of death, spouse

Civil registration records are held at local vital records offices (anyakönyvi hivatal) and the Hungarian National Archives. For records more than 90 years old, the Hungarian National Archives is the primary access point.

### Church Records (Anyakönyvek)

The primary genealogical source before 1895. Church records extend back to the 1600s in many parishes, with some going as far as the late 1500s. All major denominations kept registers:

| Denomination | Hungarian Name | Language of Records | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Catholic | Római katolikus | Latin (pre-1844), Hungarian, sometimes German | Most common. Largest denomination. |
| Reformed (Calvinist) | Református | Hungarian | Second largest. Dominant in eastern Hungary. |
| Lutheran | Evangélikus | German, Hungarian, Slovak | Common among German and Slovak minorities. |
| Greek Catholic | Görög katolikus | Latin, Hungarian, sometimes Ruthenian | Common in northeastern Hungary (now Ukraine/Slovakia). |
| Orthodox | Görög keleti | Church Slavonic, Romanian, Serbian | Common in southern and eastern Hungary. |
| Jewish | Izraelita | Hungarian, German, Hebrew | Separate registers. Some indexed on JewishGen. |
| Unitarian | Unitárius | Hungarian | Almost exclusively in Transylvania. |

FamilySearch has digitized many church record collections. Matricula Online provides access to some Catholic diocesan records.

### Multi-Language Records

A single parish might have records in multiple languages depending on the period:

- **Latin**: The official language of the Hungarian Kingdom until 1844. Most Catholic records are in Latin.
- **Hungarian**: State language after 1844. Increasingly used in all denominations.
- **German**: Common in western Hungary (now Burgenland, Austria) and in cities with significant German populations.
- **Slovak, Romanian, Croatian, Serbian**: Used in parishes serving these communities, particularly in areas now in Slovakia, Romania, Croatia, and Serbia.

This means the same record series may switch languages mid-volume. Reading skills in multiple languages are often necessary.

### Conscription Records (Összeírások)

Hungary did not have regular censuses like Western Europe. Instead, various military and tax conscriptions serve as census substitutes:

- **1715 and 1720 conscriptions**: Early national tax surveys. List heads of household.
- **1784 to 1787 census (Josephine census)**: The first modern census attempt by Joseph II. Lists all household members by name. Controversial in its time (many resisted).
- **1828 national conscription (összeírás)**: Detailed household survey. Particularly valuable for identifying families before civil registration.
- **Military conscription lists**: Various dates. List eligible males with ages, occupations, and physical descriptions.

These records are held in the Hungarian National Archives and county archives. Some are available on FamilySearch.

## Historic Hungary vs Modern Hungary

If your ancestor was "from Hungary" before 1920, they may have lived in what is now:

| Modern Country | Historic Region (Hungarian Name) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Slovakia | Upper Hungary (Felvidék) | Largest successor territory by population |
| Romania | Transylvania (Erdély) | Largest successor territory by area |
| Serbia | Vojvodina (Délvidék) | Southern lowlands |
| Croatia | Slavonia (Szlavónia) | Part of the Lands of the Crown of St. Stephen |
| Ukraine | Subcarpathia (Kárpátalja) | Northeastern corner; now Zakarpattia Oblast |
| Austria | Burgenland | Western strip transferred in 1921 |

Records from these areas may now be in the archives of any of these countries. FamilySearch often has microfilmed copies regardless of where the originals reside.

## Naming Conventions

### Hungarian Name Order
Hungarian name order is **surname first, given name second**: Nagy János = János Nagy in Western order. In records, you may see either order depending on the language: Latin and German records typically use Western order, while Hungarian-language records use Hungarian order.

### Common Surname Patterns

Many Hungarian surnames reflect the multi-ethnic nature of historic Hungary:

| Surname | Meaning | Origin |
|---|---|---|
| Nagy | Big, great | Descriptive |
| Kiss / Kis | Small, little | Descriptive |
| Szabó | Tailor | Occupational |
| Kovács | Smith | Occupational |
| Tóth | Slovak | Ethnic (indicates Slovak origin) |
| Német | German | Ethnic (indicates German origin) |
| Horvát / Horváth | Croatian | Ethnic (indicates Croatian origin) |
| Oláh | Wallachian/Romanian | Ethnic (indicates Romanian origin) |
| Varga | Cobbler | Occupational |
| Molnár | Miller | Occupational |

The prevalence of ethnic surnames (Tóth, Német, Horvát, Oláh) reflects centuries of internal migration within the multi-ethnic Kingdom of Hungary.

### Noble Name Prefixes
Noble families often used a place-based prefix: "nagybányai Horthy Miklós" indicates the Horthy family from Nagybánya. In records, the prefix may appear as a separate element or be incorporated into the surname.

## Search Strategy

1. **Determine whether the ancestor was from historic Hungary or modern Hungary.** Immigration and naturalization records may list a town name. Use a gazetteer or historical map to locate the town and determine which modern country it falls in.
2. **Identify the county (megye/vármegye).** The Hungarian county system was the primary administrative unit. Knowing the county directs you to the correct archive.
3. **Search FamilySearch** for church records from that county. Browse by denomination, parish, and year.
4. **If Catholic, check Matricula Online** for digitized parish registers from the relevant diocese.
5. **For post-1895 records**, search civil registration at the Hungarian National Archives or contact the local anyakönyvi hivatal.
6. **Check Arcanum** for newspaper references, county histories, and biographical mentions.
7. **If the location is now in another country**, search that country's archives as well. Slovak archives (porta.sk), Romanian archives (arhivelenationale.ro), and others hold records from former Hungarian territories.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming "Hungary" means modern Hungary.** Pre-1920 Hungary was roughly three times the size of today's country. Two-thirds of Hungarian genealogical research involves records now held in other countries' archives.
- **Multi-language records.** The same parish may have records in Latin, Hungarian, and German across different time periods. A researcher comfortable in one language may miss records written in another.
- **Hungarian name order.** In Hungarian-language records, the surname comes first (Nagy János). In Latin or German records from the same parish, the same person may appear as Joannes Nagy. Confusing the given name and surname is a common error.
- **Not checking successor state archives.** If your ancestor's town is now in Slovakia, Romania, Serbia, Croatia, Ukraine, or Austria, the originals may be in that country's national or regional archives. FamilySearch microfilms may provide access, but newer digitization efforts may only be available through the local country's archives.
- **Confusing county names.** Many Hungarian counties have similar names, and some counties were split or merged over the centuries. Pest county and the city of Budapest are separate entities. Zemplén county straddled what is now Hungary and Slovakia.
- **Overlooking the pre-1895 church monopoly on records.** Before October 1, 1895, there are no civil records. All vital records are in church registers, organized by denomination. You need to know not just where your ancestor lived but which church they attended.
- **Ignoring Jewish records.** Jewish communities in Hungary were large and well-documented. JewishGen, the Hungarian Jewish Archives (Magyar Zsidó Levéltár), and FamilySearch all have relevant collections. Jewish records are separate from Christian parish registers.
