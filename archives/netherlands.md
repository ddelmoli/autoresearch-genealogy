# Netherlands

The Netherlands has one of the most thoroughly digitized genealogy ecosystems in the world. Civil registration began in 1811 (under Napoleonic rule), and most records are freely accessible online. Over 220 million genealogical entries are indexed. The combination of civil registration, population registers, and church records creates an unusually complete picture of Dutch families from the 1600s onward.

## Key Databases

### WieWasWie
- **URL**: https://www.wiewaswie.nl/
- **Coverage**: Central search portal for Dutch genealogy. Indexes civil registration, church records, and population registers from many archives across the Netherlands.
- **Cost**: Free/Freemium
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, date, event type, location)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. Returns structured results. English interface available.

### OpenArchives
- **URL**: https://www.openarch.nl/
- **Coverage**: Aggregated index of Dutch archival records. Over 100 million records from participating archives.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, date, event type)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. Structured results.

### FamilySearch (Netherlands collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Large indexed and image collections. Many Dutch records available including civil registration, church records, and population registers.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **Searchable**: Varies by collection (some indexed, many browse-only)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for images

### Alle Groningers
- **URL**: https://www.allegroningers.nl/
- **Coverage**: Complete genealogical database for the province of Groningen. Civil registration, church records, population registers.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, date, location)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

### Delpher
- **URL**: https://www.delpher.nl/
- **Coverage**: Royal Library's digitized newspapers, books, and periodicals. Millions of pages with full-text OCR search.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Full-text search
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. Article text is readable.
- **Key use**: Obituaries, birth and marriage announcements, family notices, biographical mentions.

### Ancestry.com
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.com/
- **Coverage**: Some Dutch indexed collections including civil registration and emigration records.
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### Regionaal Archief (various)
- **Coverage**: Each region has its own archive website with digitized records. Examples include Stadsarchief Amsterdam, Regionaal Archief Tilburg, Het Utrechts Archief, and many others.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Varies by archive
- **AI accessibility**: Varies by archive. Many offer structured search, some are browse-only images.

## Record Types

### Civil Registration (Burgerlijke Stand)

Civil registration began in 1811 under Napoleonic rule. Three types of records:

- **Geboorteakten** (birth records): Name, date, parents, witnesses
- **Huwelijksakten** (marriage records): The most detailed Dutch record type. Include both parties' names, ages, occupations, birthplaces, parents' names (with status: living or deceased), occupations, and witnesses. Marriage annexes (bijlagen) are filed with the marriage record and may contain birth certificates, death certificates of deceased parents, military service proof, and parental consent documents.
- **Overlijdensakten** (death records): Name, age, occupation, spouse, parents, place of death

Marriage records are the single most informative document type in Dutch genealogy. The annexes alone can contain documents spanning decades and multiple family members.

### Population Registers (Bevolkingsregisters)

From 1850 onward. These are essentially continuous censuses: every household was registered, and all changes (births, deaths, marriages, moves in, moves out) were recorded in real time. Each register page tracks a household across an entire decade.

Population registers record:
- Every person in a household (name, birthdate, birthplace, religion, occupation)
- Relationships within the household
- Date of arrival and departure (with destination)
- Marriages and deaths within the registration period

This system is uniquely Dutch and incredibly valuable. It allows you to follow a family from household to household, municipality to municipality, across decades. Available for most municipalities through regional archives.

### Church Records (Kerkelijke Registers)

Before 1811, churches maintained all vital records. Denominations include:

| Denomination | Dutch Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Dutch Reformed | Hervormde Kerk | Most common. State church until 1796. Most extensive registers. |
| Catholic | Rooms-Katholiek | Common in the south (Brabant, Limburg). Less complete in the north. |
| Lutheran | Luthers | Common in cities, especially Amsterdam. |
| Mennonite | Doopsgezind | No infant baptism, so baptism records start at adult age. |
| Jewish | Joods | Separate registers. Many indexed on JewishGen. |
| Walloon | Waals | French-speaking Protestant. Records in French. |

Coverage varies by parish and denomination. Dutch Reformed registers are the most common and often the most complete.

## The 1811 Surname Law

Under Napoleonic rule, all Dutch citizens were required to register a fixed hereditary surname. Families that had been using patronymics suddenly had to choose a permanent surname. This created several patterns:

- Some kept the patronymic as a surname (Jansen = son of Jan, Pietersen = son of Pieter)
- Some chose place names (van den Berg, van Dijk)
- Some chose occupational names (Bakker = baker, Visser = fisherman)
- Some chose descriptive or humorous names (legend has it some families chose absurd names because they did not believe the law would last)

The law was enforced most strictly in the northern provinces. In the south (Brabant, Limburg), many families already had hereditary surnames from earlier.

## Naming Conventions

### Patronymics
Patronymics were used in some regions (particularly Friesland and parts of the northern provinces) until the 1811 surname law. Under the patronymic system, a child took their father's first name as a surname: Jan Pieterszoon = Jan, son of Pieter. The next generation would have a different surname based on their father's name.

### Surname Patterns
- **van** ("from"): Indicates geographic origin (van Amsterdam, van den Berg)
- **de/het** ("the"): Article prefix (de Groot, de Vries)
- **-sen/-zoon** ("son of"): Patronymic origin (Janssen, Pieterzoon)
- **-ma** (Frisian): Frisian patronymic suffix (Dijkema, Sikkema)
- **-stra** (Frisian): Frisian suffix indicating origin (Dijkstra = from the dike)
- **-ink/-ing**: Eastern Dutch patronymic suffix (Berendink)

### Frisian Names
Friesland has its own naming traditions and language. Frisian given names (Douwe, Sjoerd, Rienk, Tjitske) are distinct from Dutch names. Frisian patronymic suffixes (-ma, -stra, -sma) are immediately recognizable.

## Search Strategy

1. Start with **WieWasWie** for civil registration (births, marriages, deaths from 1811 onward). Marriage records are the most informative.
2. Check **population registers** (bevolkingsregisters) for household composition and movements. These are available through regional archives and often through WieWasWie.
3. Search **church records** for pre-1811 research. Dutch Reformed and Catholic registers are the most common.
4. Use **Delpher** for newspaper mentions: obituaries, marriage announcements, business notices.
5. Check **regional archives** for additional collections not indexed in WieWasWie or OpenArchives.
6. For provincial research, check dedicated databases like **Alle Groningers** for Groningen.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Confusing the Netherlands with Holland.** Holland is only 2 of 12 provinces (North Holland and South Holland). Using "Holland" to refer to the entire country may cause you to miss records from other provinces, and it makes Dutch people wince.
- **Not using population registers.** The bevolkingsregisters are the most unique and valuable Dutch source. No other country maintained anything comparable. They track families continuously and record every move, birth, death, and marriage within a household.
- **Pre-1811 patronymics in the northern provinces.** Before the surname law, surnames changed every generation. Jan Pieterszoon's son might be Hendrik Janszoon. Tracing a family backward through patronymics requires attention to given names, not surnames.
- **Multiple archive systems.** Dutch records are held across national, provincial, regional, and municipal archives. WieWasWie and OpenArchives aggregate many but not all of these. A negative search in WieWasWie does not mean the record does not exist; check the relevant regional archive directly.
- **Assuming records are in Dutch.** French was the administrative language during Napoleonic rule (1795 to 1813). Early civil registration records may be in French. Church records from Walloon congregations are in French. Some records in border areas are in German.
- **Overlooking marriage annexes (bijlagen).** The documents attached to marriage records (birth certificates, death certificates of parents, military records) are often filed separately and can contain information not available anywhere else.
