# Spain and Portugal

Iberian Peninsula genealogy benefits from centuries of meticulous Catholic record keeping and, uniquely, from the Inquisition archives, which contain some of the most detailed genealogical investigations ever compiled. Both countries had vast colonial empires, meaning family research often spans multiple continents. Records are fragmented across national, regional, diocesan, and municipal archives.

## Key Databases (Spain)

### PARES (Portal de Archivos Españoles)
- **URL**: https://pares.mcu.es/
- **Coverage**: Spanish national archives portal. Documents from the Archivo General de Indias (colonial Americas), Archivo Historico Nacional, Archivo General de Simancas, and others. Millions of digitized documents.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (browse and keyword search)
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-searchable (Spanish language)

### FamilySearch (Spain collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Large collection of Catholic parish records and civil registration from across Spain. Many indexed, many browse-only image sets.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **Searchable**: Varies by collection (some indexed, many browse-only)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for images

### Ancestry.com (Spain collections)
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.com/
- **Coverage**: Some indexed Spanish collections including selected civil and church records
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### Catastro de Ensenada (1749 to 1756)
- **Coverage**: Detailed census of the Crown of Castile ordered by the Marquis of Ensenada. Lists head of household, occupation, property, family members, and livestock. One of the most comprehensive pre-modern censuses in Europe.
- **Cost**: Free (digitized in PARES)
- **Searchable**: Browse-only within PARES
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only. Image-based, Spanish language.

### Regional Archives (Comunidades Autonomas)
- **Coverage**: Each Comunidad Autonoma has its own archive system with significant holdings. Catalonia (Arxiu Nacional de Catalunya), Basque Country (Irargi), Galicia (Arquivo de Galicia), and Andalusia (Archivo General de Andalucia) have the most extensive online collections.
- **Cost**: Free (varies by region)
- **Searchable**: Varies by archive
- **AI accessibility**: Varies. Most are browser-only with image-based records.

## Key Databases (Portugal)

### Torre do Tombo / ANTT
- **URL**: https://digitarq.arquivos.pt/
- **Coverage**: Portuguese national archives. Digitized parish records (registos paroquiais), Inquisition records, notarial records, and overseas records from former colonies.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (catalog searchable, records are image-based)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (Portuguese language, image-based)

### FamilySearch (Portugal collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Extensive collection of Portuguese parish records, civil registration, and passport records. Strong coverage for the Azores and Madeira.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **Searchable**: Varies by collection
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for images

### Ancestry.com (Portugal collections)
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.com/
- **Coverage**: Some Portuguese collections including selected civil and church records
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

## Civil Registration

### Spain
Civil registration (Registro Civil) began in 1871. Before that date, Catholic parish records are the primary source for births, marriages, and deaths. Compliance with civil registration was uneven in the early years, particularly in rural areas.

### Portugal
Civil registration began in 1911 with the establishment of the Republic. Before that, Catholic parish records effectively served this function. For the Azores and Madeira, church records extend back to the 1500s and are often the only available source.

In both countries, church records are the primary source for all research before civil registration.

## Church Records

Catholic parish records (registros parroquiales in Spanish, registos paroquiais in Portuguese) are the backbone of Iberian genealogy. Coverage begins in the 1500s following the Council of Trent (1563), though some parishes have records from even earlier.

Three record types:
- **Baptisms** (bautismos / baptismos): Name, date, parents, godparents (padrinos), legitimacy status
- **Marriages** (matrimonios / casamentos): Names and parents of both parties, witnesses, dispensations for consanguinity
- **Burials** (defunciones / obitos): Name, age, sometimes parents and spouse

In Spain, many parish records are held at diocesan archives rather than the parishes themselves. In Portugal, most have been centralized and digitized at Torre do Tombo.

## The Inquisition Records

Both Spain (from 1478) and Portugal (from 1536) maintained extensive Inquisition archives. These are paradoxically among the best genealogical sources for Sephardic Jewish, Morisco (Muslim convert), and other minority families.

Inquisition tribunals conducted genealogical investigations called limpieza de sangre (Spanish) or limpeza de sangue (Portuguese), meaning "purity of blood." These investigations traced family lineages back multiple generations to identify conversos (Jewish or Muslim converts to Christianity). The resulting documents often contain:
- Multi-generational family trees
- Places of origin
- Occupations
- Depositions from neighbors and associates
- Details about religious practices

Spanish Inquisition records are held at the Archivo Historico Nacional (searchable via PARES). Portuguese Inquisition records are held at Torre do Tombo and are largely digitized.

## The Colonial Connection

Both Spain and Portugal had vast colonial empires. If your ancestor emigrated to Latin America, Africa, or Asia, records may exist in both the home country and the destination.

**Spain**: The Archivo General de Indias in Seville holds passenger lists, emigration permits, colonial administration records, and correspondence for all of Spanish America and the Philippines. These records cover the period from 1492 through the colonial era and are searchable through PARES.

**Portugal**: Torre do Tombo holds records for Brazil, Angola, Mozambique, Goa, Macau, and other former colonies. The overseas archives (Arquivo Historico Ultramarino) are a separate collection focused on colonial administration.

## Naming Conventions

### Spain
Two surnames: father's first surname + mother's first surname. Maria Garcia Lopez means Maria, father's surname Garcia, mother's surname Lopez. In older records, "de" may appear between given name and surname (Juan de Garcia). Women did not traditionally change their surnames at marriage.

### Portugal
Can have multiple surnames; the order is typically mother's surname then father's surname (opposite of Spain). "Da," "dos," "de" are common particles that may or may not be capitalized. Like Spain, women traditionally kept their own surnames.

### Basque Naming
Basque names follow different conventions from Castilian. Many Basque surnames derive from the farmhouse (baserri) where the family lived, not from a patronymic or descriptive origin. Names like Etxeberria ("new house"), Goikoetxea ("upper house"), and Mendizabal ("wide mountain") are geographic.

## Search Strategy (Spain)

1. **Identify the province and municipality** from emigration records, family knowledge, or surname distribution tools
2. **Search FamilySearch** for Catholic parish records from that parish
3. **Check PARES** for holdings at the national archive level (particularly Archivo Historico Nacional and Archivo General de Simancas)
4. **Check the regional archive** for the Comunidad Autonoma
5. **For colonial ancestors**, search the Archivo General de Indias via PARES
6. **Search for Inquisition records** if the family has Sephardic, Morisco, or converso connections

## Search Strategy (Portugal)

1. **Identify the distrito and concelho** from emigration records, family knowledge, or passport records
2. **Search Torre do Tombo** (digitarq.arquivos.pt) for parish records from that parish
3. **Search FamilySearch** for indexed or browse-only collections
4. **For Brazilian connections**, check both Portuguese and Brazilian archives (Arquivo Nacional do Brasil at https://www.gov.br/arquivonacional/)
5. **For Azores and Madeira research**, FamilySearch has particularly strong coverage

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming all records are at a central archive.** Spanish records are fragmented across diocesan, municipal, regional, and national archives. A single family's records may be split across four or five institutions.
- **The two-surname system confusing foreign researchers.** In both countries, the "last name" as understood in English-speaking countries is actually two surnames. The patronymic surname (father's side) is listed first in Spain but second in Portugal.
- **Confusing Galician records with Portuguese.** Galician and Portuguese are closely related languages, and the border region has overlapping record sets. A family from northern Portugal may have connections in Galicia and vice versa.
- **Inquisition-era naming conventions.** Converso families sometimes changed their names to Catholic ones. A family that was "Cohen" might appear as "Santa Cruz" in post-conversion records.
- **Basque naming conventions differ from Castilian.** Basque surnames are geographic, not patronymic, and the Basque language (Euskera) is unrelated to Spanish. Standard Spanish genealogical assumptions do not apply in the Basque Country.
- **Not searching for Inquisition records.** Many researchers do not realize that limpieza de sangre investigations are available and searchable. These contain some of the most detailed genealogical information in European archives.
- **Overlooking the Canary Islands and Azores.** Both archipelagos have distinct record sets and were major waypoints for emigration to the Americas. Canary Islands records are in Spanish regional archives; Azores records are on FamilySearch and Torre do Tombo.
