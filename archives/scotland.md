# Scotland

Scottish genealogical records are generally considered better organized than English ones, thanks to a centralized vital records system and the exceptional detail of statutory registration from 1855. The primary gateway is ScotlandsPeople, the official government genealogy service.

## Key Databases

### ScotlandsPeople
- **URL**: https://www.scotlandspeople.gov.uk/
- **Coverage**: Statutory registers of births, marriages, and deaths (1855+); Old Parochial Records (OPRs, some from 1553); census records (1841 to 1921); wills and testaments (1513+); Catholic parish registers; divorce records; coats of arms
- **Cost**: Pay-per-view credits system (£7 for 30 page credits). Index searches are free; viewing images costs credits.
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, date range, parish, event type)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (requires login and credit purchase to view images)
- **Note**: This is the single most important resource for Scottish genealogy. Most other platforms have only a subset of what ScotlandsPeople holds.

### National Records of Scotland (NRS)
- **URL**: https://www.nrscotland.gov.uk/
- **Coverage**: The government authority behind ScotlandsPeople. Physical archives in Edinburgh hold original records and collections not yet digitized.
- **Cost**: Free to search catalogs; reading room access is free with registration
- **AI accessibility**: Catalog is AI-readable

### FamilySearch (Scotland collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Some indexed Scottish church and civil records, but significantly less comprehensive than ScotlandsPeople
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for individual records

### FindMyPast
- **URL**: https://www.findmypast.co.uk/
- **Coverage**: Scottish newspaper archives (British Newspaper Archive), some parish records
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### Ancestry
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.co.uk/
- **Coverage**: Some Scottish collections including census transcriptions and military records
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)
- **Note**: For Scottish research, ScotlandsPeople is almost always the better primary source. Ancestry and FindMyPast are supplementary.

## Statutory Registration (1855 onward)

Scottish civil registration began on January 1, 1855. Scottish statutory records are exceptionally detailed, particularly in the first year.

### The remarkable 1855 records
The 1855 records collected far more information than any other year. The government deemed the extra detail too burdensome on registrars and scaled it back from 1856 onward. Some of the extra fields were reintroduced in 1861.

| Field | 1855 | 1856 to 1860 | 1861 onward |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Birth records** | Parents' ages, marriage date, other living and deceased children | Standard fields only | Parents' marriage date restored |
| **Marriage records** | Birthplaces of both parties, current addresses, children (living and deceased) | Standard fields only | Some extra fields restored |
| **Death records** | Birthplace, parents' names, spouse's name, burial place | Standard fields only | Some extra fields restored |

This means an 1855 birth record can effectively reconstruct an entire family's vital statistics in a single document.

### Access periods
Records become available on ScotlandsPeople after statutory closure periods:
- Births: 100 years
- Marriages: 75 years
- Deaths: 50 years

## Old Parochial Records (OPRs)

The Old Parochial Records are Church of Scotland (Presbyterian) parish registers predating statutory registration. They record baptisms, proclamations of marriage (banns), and burials.

- **Date range**: Some parishes from 1553, but most have reliable coverage only from the 1700s
- **Coverage**: Uneven. Lowland parishes tend to have better survival than Highland ones. Some parishes have gaps of decades.
- **Content**: Varies widely. Some ministers recorded detailed entries; others wrote minimal information.
- **Access**: Indexed and available on ScotlandsPeople

OPRs are the primary source for pre-1855 Scottish genealogy. For Catholic families, separate Catholic parish registers (also on ScotlandsPeople) cover some parishes from the late 1700s.

## Census Records

Scottish censuses follow the same decennial schedule as England and Wales:

| Year | Notes |
|---|---|
| 1841 | First useful census. Ages rounded for adults. Limited birthplace info. |
| 1851 | Exact ages, birthplace (parish and county), relationship to head. |
| 1861 | Similar to 1851. Number of rooms with windows recorded. |
| 1871 | Similar detail. Gaelic language ability recorded. |
| 1881 | Well-indexed. |
| 1891 | Gaelic and English language ability. |
| 1901 | Similar detail. |
| 1911 | Original household schedules survive. Fertility data for married women. |
| 1921 | Released 2022 (via ScotlandsPeople, not FindMyPast as in England). |

## Wills and Testaments

Scottish wills and testaments (from 1513 onward) are available on ScotlandsPeople. The Scottish legal system for inheritance differs from English law:

- **Testament Testamentar**: Where the deceased left a will
- **Testament Dative**: Where no will existed (intestate); the court appointed an executor
- **Inventory**: List of the deceased's moveable property and debts

These records often name family members, creditors, and business associates, making them valuable for establishing relationships even when church records are missing.

## Scots-Irish Distinction

"Scots-Irish" or "Ulster Scots" refers to Scottish Presbyterians (mostly Lowland Scots) who settled in Ulster (Northern Ireland) during the Plantation of Ulster in the 1600s. Many of their descendants later emigrated to America in the 1700s and 1800s.

Key points for research:
- Records may exist in Scotland (pre-migration), Northern Ireland (PRONI at https://www.nidirect.gov.uk/proni), or both
- Do not confuse Scots-Irish ancestry with native Irish ancestry; the migration routes, religions, and record sets are different
- Scots-Irish families who emigrated to America often arrived through Philadelphia or the ports of the Delaware Valley, not through New York or Boston

## Naming Conventions

### Scottish Naming Pattern
Scottish families traditionally followed a predictable naming pattern for children:

1. First son: named after the paternal grandfather
2. Second son: named after the maternal grandfather
3. Third son: named after the father
4. First daughter: named after the maternal grandmother
5. Second daughter: named after the paternal grandmother
6. Third daughter: named after the mother

This pattern is a useful guide for predicting names when records are missing. If you find a couple's first son is named "James" and second son "Robert," you can hypothesize that the paternal grandfather was James and the maternal grandfather was Robert, then search accordingly.

**Caveat**: This is a common pattern, not a rule. Families deviated for many reasons (honoring a deceased sibling, a wealthy relative, or personal preference). Use it as a guide, never as proof.

### Mac/Mc Prefix
Same usage as in Irish naming: "son of." Mac and Mc are interchangeable abbreviations. MacDonald, McDonald, and Macdonald are all the same name.

### Clan Names
In the Highlands, clan affiliation could produce clusters of the same surname in a region (Cameron, Campbell, MacDonald, MacLeod, etc.). As with Welsh Jones and Davies, location and parentage are essential for distinguishing individuals.

## Search Strategy

1. Start with ScotlandsPeople for post-1855 statutory records (births, marriages, deaths)
2. Search census records (1841 to 1921) on ScotlandsPeople for household composition and birthplace information
3. For pre-1855, search Old Parochial Records on ScotlandsPeople for the identified parish
4. Search wills and testaments (1513+) on ScotlandsPeople for family relationships and property details
5. For Scots-Irish connections, check PRONI for Ulster records
6. Use FindMyPast for newspaper archives to supplement official records

## Common Pitfalls

- **ScotlandsPeople costs money.** Unlike the free GRO index (FreeBMD) for England, viewing Scottish records requires purchasing credits. Budget accordingly. Index searches are free, so search the index first to confirm a record exists before spending credits.
- **Records on ScotlandsPeople may not appear elsewhere.** Many Scottish records digitized on ScotlandsPeople are exclusive to that platform and do not appear on Ancestry or FamilySearch. Do not assume that a negative result on Ancestry means the record does not exist.
- **The naming pattern is a guide, not a rule.** Predicting a grandfather's name from the naming pattern is a useful hypothesis, but it must be confirmed with documentary evidence. Families deviated from the pattern regularly.
- **Old Style dates.** Scotland adopted the Gregorian calendar in 1752. Before that, the year began on March 25 (Lady Day), not January 1. A date recorded as "15 February 1740" in the old system is actually February 1741 in modern reckoning. Many OPR transcriptions normalize dates, but some do not.
- **Highland vs. Lowland record survival.** Highland parish records are sparser and begin later than Lowland records. Some Highland parishes have no OPRs before the late 1700s.
- **Confusing ScotlandsPeople with Scotland's People (possessive).** The official URL is https://www.scotlandspeople.gov.uk/ with no apostrophe.
