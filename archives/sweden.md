# Sweden

Swedish church records are extraordinarily detailed, maintained continuously since the 1600s, and increasingly digitized.

## Key Databases

### Riksarkivet (National Archives of Sweden)
- **URL**: https://sok.riksarkivet.se/
- **Coverage**: Indexed birth, marriage, death records; census records; military records
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (person search)
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-searchable. Some searches blocked by CAPTCHA.
- **Languages**: Swedish (English option for some tools)

### Arkiv Digital
- **URL**: https://www.arkivdigital.se/
- **Coverage**: 110M+ images of Swedish church records, estate inventories, court records
- **Cost**: Subscription (SEK 1,695/year or SEK 195/month)
- **Searchable**: Indexed birth records (1800 to 1840); other records are browse-only images
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)
- **Key use**: The most comprehensive digitized collection of Swedish church records

### SVAR (Svensk Arkivinformation)
- **URL**: Part of Riksarkivet (https://sok.riksarkivet.se/)
- **Coverage**: Microfilmed Swedish church records
- **Cost**: Free for basic access
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only

### Emihamn (Swedish Emigration Database)
- **URL**: https://emiweb.se/
- **Coverage**: Port emigration records from 6 Swedish ports, 1869 to 1950 (1.4M names)
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable
- **Note**: Only covers departures from 1869 onward. Earlier emigration may only appear in church "moving out" records.

### Swedish Emigrant Institute (Emigrantinstitutet)
- **URL**: Located in Växjö, Sweden
- **Coverage**: Emigrant letters, diaries, photographs, research library
- **Cost**: On-site access
- **AI accessibility**: In-person only

## Swedish Church Records (Kyrkoböcker)

Swedish church records are uniquely valuable because they include "household examination rolls" (husförhörslängder), which are essentially annual censuses of every household in a parish, maintained by the local minister. These record:
- Every person in the household
- Birth date and birthplace
- Reading ability (literacy assessment)
- Communion attendance
- Moves in and out of the parish (inflyttning/utflyttning)

**Moving records (flyttningsbetyg)** are critical for tracking ancestors across parishes. When someone moved, the pastor recorded where they went, creating a chain you can follow.

## Search Strategy

1. Start with Riksarkivet person search for your known ancestor
2. If found, note the parish (församling) and county (län)
3. Search for the household examination roll (husförhörslängd) for that parish
4. Follow the moving records (utflyttningslängd) to track where the family went
5. If they emigrated, check Emihamn for port departure records
6. Search Arkiv Digital (subscription) for original church record images

## Naming Conventions

- Swedish patronymics: Andersson (son of Anders), Andersdotter (daughter of Anders)
- Soldier names: Men conscripted into the military were given unique names to avoid confusion (e.g., Modig, Stolt, Ljung)
- Farm names were less commonly used as surnames in Sweden compared to Norway
- The 1901 Name Law required Swedes to adopt fixed hereditary surnames
