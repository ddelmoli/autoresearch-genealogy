# Example: DNA to Genealogy Mapping

How to connect consumer DNA test results to documented ancestor lines.

## Starting Data

All names, percentages, and line assignments in this example are synthetic.

The researcher has:
- Provider A results: 52% Northern European, 21% Eastern European, 14% Central European, 6% Ashkenazi Jewish, 7% Broadly European
- Provider B results: 49% Northern European, 19% Baltic/Eastern European, 18% Central European, 5% Ashkenazi Jewish, 9% Other
- Documented tree: 2 Northern European grandparent lines, 1 Eastern European grandparent line, 1 Central European grandparent line

## Step 1: Identify Strong Signals

**~50% Northern European**: Both providers broadly agree (52% vs 49%). Two documented grandparent lines are Northern European. This is a Strong Signal. The proportion is consistent with two of four grandparent lines, allowing for recombination and mixed ancestry within each line.

The exact percentage should not be over-read. A documented grandparent line does not contribute exactly 25% in every generation, and provider categories do not map cleanly to historical borders.

**~5 to 6% Ashkenazi Jewish**: Both providers report the same distinctive population signal. This is likely real, not noise. But no ancestor in the tree is documented as Jewish. This opens an investigation: which ancestor line carries this signal?

## Step 2: Apply the Assignment Framework

For the Ashkenazi signal:
1. **Does genealogical evidence predict it?** Not directly. But one documented line traces to a multi-religious Central European city where conversion and intermarriage are plausible historical pathways.
2. **Is the magnitude plausible?** 5 to 6% is consistent with one great-great-grandparent being fully Ashkenazi, or partial Ashkenazi ancestry across multiple lines.
3. **Are there alternatives?** Several documented lines remain possible. Do not assign the signal to one line without stronger evidence.
4. **Can phasing confirm?** Without a parent's test, assignment is probabilistic. If a parent or close relative tests, segment inheritance can narrow the side of the tree.

**Conclusion**: The signal is real, but the ancestor line is not proven. The right next step is targeted documentary research and, if possible, parent or close-relative DNA testing.

## Step 3: Address Moderate Signals

**~14 to 18% Central European**: The providers broadly agree but label the component differently. This is a Moderate Signal: the ancestry is real, but the exact percentage and label are uncertain because neighboring populations overlap genetically.

**Small trace component**: One provider reports a small trace ancestry component that does not appear in the documented tree. Treat it as a lead only if it appears across providers or matches a documentary clue.

## Lessons

1. DNA confirms broad ancestry composition but cannot pinpoint specific ancestors without additional evidence
2. The X chromosome (for males) is a powerful tool for separating maternal from paternal ancestry
3. Provider disagreements usually reflect different categorization, not different DNA
4. Small percentages (<5%) should not drive research decisions
5. The most interesting finding (Ashkenazi signal) required genealogical investigation to interpret
