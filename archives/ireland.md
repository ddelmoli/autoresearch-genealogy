# Ireland

Irish genealogical research is uniquely difficult. The 1922 Four Courts fire destroyed centuries of Church of Ireland parish registers and nearly all pre-1901 census returns. The 1821 to 1851 censuses were pulped by government order. This catastrophic loss creates heavy reliance on "census substitutes" and makes every surviving record more valuable.

## Key Databases

### IrishGenealogy.ie
- **URL**: https://www.irishgenealogy.ie/
- **Coverage**: Civil registration: births (1864+), marriages (1845+ for non-Catholic, 1864+ for all denominations), deaths (1864+). Church records from some parishes.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, date range, event type, registration district)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. Results are structured HTML.

### Census 1901 and 1911
- **URL**: http://www.census.nationalarchives.ie/
- **Coverage**: The only surviving complete Irish censuses. Household returns with names, ages, occupations, religion, literacy, birthplace (county), and Irish language ability.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, townland, county)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. Both index and original images available.

### National Library of Ireland Catholic Parish Registers
- **URL**: https://registers.nli.ie/
- **Coverage**: Microfilmed Catholic parish registers, mostly 1740s to 1880s. Baptisms, marriages, and some burials.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Browse-only (navigate by diocese and parish)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only. Records are scanned microfilm images.

### Griffith's Valuation
- **URL**: https://www.askaboutireland.ie/griffith-valuation/
- **Coverage**: 1847 to 1864 property valuation covering all of Ireland. Lists every head of household, the townland, landlord, and property description.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by surname and county/parish)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. Results are structured tables.
- **Note**: The single most important census substitute for Irish research.

### Tithe Applotment Books (1820s to 1830s)
- **Coverage**: Pre-famine record of land occupiers liable for tithes. Covers most of rural Ireland.
- **Cost**: Free (available on FamilySearch and FindMyPast)
- **Searchable**: Indexed on FindMyPast; browse-only images on FamilySearch
- **AI accessibility**: Varies by platform

### HistoricGraves.com
- **URL**: https://www.historicgraves.com/
- **Coverage**: Community-sourced graveyard inscription database across Ireland
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, graveyard, county)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

### FindMyPast
- **URL**: https://www.findmypast.ie/
- **Coverage**: Largest subscription collection of Irish records. Includes 1901 and 1911 censuses, Griffith's Valuation, Tithe Applotment Books, Catholic parish registers, landed estates court records, newspapers.
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### Ancestry.com
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.com/
- **Coverage**: Irish collections including Landed Estates Court records, some indexed civil and church records
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### PRONI (Public Record Office of Northern Ireland)
- **URL**: https://www.nidirect.gov.uk/proni
- **Coverage**: Records for the six counties of Northern Ireland (Antrim, Armagh, Derry, Down, Fermanagh, Tyrone). Church records, estate papers, valuation records.
- **Cost**: Free online access to some collections
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-readable

### FamilySearch (Ireland collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Indexed civil registration, some church records, Tithe Applotment Books
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for individual records

## Census Substitutes

Since the pre-1901 censuses are destroyed, Irish researchers must reconstruct family information from alternative sources. These "census substitutes" are the backbone of pre-1901 Irish research:

| Source | Date Range | What It Records |
|---|---|---|
| Griffith's Valuation | 1847 to 1864 | Head of household, townland, landlord, property description |
| Tithe Applotment Books | 1820s to 1830s | Land occupiers liable for church tithes |
| Hearth Money Rolls | 1660s | Households taxed by number of hearths |
| Flax Growers Lists | 1796 | Farmers who received spinning wheel premiums |
| Freeholders Lists | Various (1700s to 1800s) | Men eligible to vote based on property |
| Petty Sessions Court Records | 1850s onward | Local court proceedings (names, addresses, occupations) |
| Landed Estates Court Rentals | 1850s to 1880s | Tenants on bankrupt estates |

## The Townland Problem

Irish records are organized by a geographic hierarchy: **townland, parish, barony, county, province**. The townland is the smallest unit; there are roughly 60,000 townlands in Ireland, many sharing names across different counties.

If you do not know which townland your ancestor lived in, finding records is extremely difficult. The 1901 and 1911 censuses are searchable by name and are the best starting point for identifying the townland. Once you have the townland, you can search Griffith's Valuation for the same location and work backward through earlier records.

The Townlands Index (https://www.townlands.ie/) maps every townland to its parish, barony, county, and poor law union.

## Naming Conventions

### Gaelic Prefixes
- **O'** (Ó): "grandson of" or "descendant of" (Ó Briain = descendant of Brian)
- **Mac/Mc**: "son of" (Mac Carthaigh = son of Carthach)

### Anglicization
Irish names were systematically anglicized over centuries. The same Gaelic name can appear in many English forms:

- Ó Suilleabháin became O'Sullivan, then Sullivan
- Mac Giolla Phádraig became Fitzpatrick or Kilpatrick
- Ó Murchú became Murphy or Morchoe

A single family might appear as O'Sullivan in one record, Sullivan in the next, and even "Silvane" in an English-language document. Always search for multiple spelling variants.

### Townland as Identifier
In rural Ireland, people were often distinguished by their townland rather than by a unique surname. "Patrick Murphy of Ballynahinch" and "Patrick Murphy of Knockanore" were two different people, even in the same parish.

## Search Strategy

1. Start with the 1901 and 1911 census at http://www.census.nationalarchives.ie/ to locate your ancestor's townland
2. Search Griffith's Valuation for the same townland (1847 to 1864) to find the previous generation
3. Identify the Catholic diocese for that townland, then search parish registers at https://registers.nli.ie/
4. Check IrishGenealogy.ie for civil registration records (births, marriages, deaths from 1864+)
5. Search Tithe Applotment Books for the same townland (1820s to 1830s) for an earlier generation
6. For Northern Ireland families, check PRONI collections in parallel

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming all Irish records are destroyed.** The Four Courts fire was devastating, but many records survive: Catholic parish registers (most were held locally, not at the Four Courts), civil registration from 1864, Griffith's Valuation, the 1901 and 1911 censuses, and wills proved at district registries.
- **Confusing townlands with the same name.** There are multiple townlands called "Ballymore," "Kilmore," and "Knocknagree" across different counties. Always confirm the county and parish.
- **Not knowing which diocese a parish belongs to.** Catholic diocesan boundaries do not follow county boundaries. A parish in County Cork might belong to the Diocese of Cloyne, Cork and Ross, or Kerry. The NLI register viewer organizes by diocese, not county.
- **Overlooking Northern Ireland records at PRONI.** If your ancestor came from any of the six counties, PRONI holds extensive records that may not appear in Republic of Ireland databases.
- **Ignoring the Famine gap.** The Great Famine (1845 to 1852) caused massive emigration, death, and displacement. Families present in Tithe Applotment Books (1820s to 1830s) may be absent from Griffith's Valuation (1847 to 1864) because they died, emigrated, or moved.
- **Searching by surname alone.** In areas with high surname concentration (Sullivan in Kerry, Murphy in Cork, Kelly in Galway), you must combine surname with townland, parish, or spouse's name to get useful results.
