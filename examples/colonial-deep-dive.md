# Example: Colonial Deep Dive

How to research a colonial American ancestor with limited records.

## Starting Point

The tree shows a 5th great-grandfather: "[ANCESTOR] [SURNAME]" (b. ~1750, d. before 1791, [COUNTY], [COLONY/STATE]). Known facts:
- Swore an oath of allegiance in 1778
- Provided wheat for military use in 1782
- Died before June 13, 1791 (date of his estate inventory)
- Wife's name: unknown

## Research Process

### Search 1: Published County Histories

**Query**: `"[ANCESTOR] [SURNAME]" [COUNTY] colonial`
**Result**: Found in a published compilation of colonial records. Entry confirms the oath of allegiance and wheat provision. Also lists two other men with the same surname who swore oaths on the same day, suggesting family (father and sons, or brothers).

**Extracted facts**:
- Oath sworn at "[LOCATION], [COUNTY]"
- The other two [SURNAME] men: [RELATIVE-1] and [RELATIVE-2]

### Search 2: Family Relationship

**Query**: `"[RELATIVE-1]" "[RELATIVE-2]" [COUNTY] [SURNAME]`
**Result**: A published genealogy identifies [RELATIVE-1] as [ANCESTOR]'s father (b. 1720, d. before 1791) and [RELATIVE-2] as a brother or cousin (militia records show same unit, same rank).

**Evaluation**: Published genealogy is Tier 2. The co-occurrence on the same oath list at the same location supports the family connection. Accepted with note.

### Search 3: Land Records

**Query**: `[ANCESTOR] [SURNAME] land [COUNTY] [STATE]`
**Result**: FamilySearch has digitized deed books for this county. Found a deed referencing "[ANCESTOR] [SURNAME]" receiving land from "[RELATIVE-1] [SURNAME]" (consistent with father-to-son transfer).

**New information**: The deed includes a metes and bounds description, placing the family in a specific part of the county.

### Search 4: Wife's Name

**Query**: `[ANCESTOR] [SURNAME] wife [COUNTY]`
**Result**: No direct result. Probate records might list the wife, but the county probate books are not digitized.

**Query**: `[ANCESTOR] [SURNAME] probate [COUNTY]`
**Result**: The published county history mentions the estate inventory date (June 13, 1791) but does not name an administrator or wife.

**Logged as negative result**: Wife's name not findable through free online sources. Added to Open Questions with next step: contact county courthouse for probate records.

### Search 5: DAR Eligibility

The 1778 oath and 1782 wheat provision constitute "Patriotic Service" per DAR standards. This ancestor qualifies for DAR membership for descendants.

## Outcome

- **New individuals added**: 2 ([RELATIVE-1] as father, [RELATIVE-2] as possible sibling)
- **New generation**: Tree extended back to ~1720
- **Sources cited**: Published county history (Tier 2), deed book (Tier 1), DAR Patriot Index
- **Unresolved**: Wife's name (requires courthouse research)
- **Bonus finding**: DAR eligibility established

## Colonial Research Lessons

1. Published county histories are often the first and best source for colonial ancestors
2. Look for co-occurring surnames in the same records (oaths, militia, land) to identify family groups
3. Land records are among the best-surviving colonial records
4. Women's names are the hardest colonial-era facts to find; probate and church records are the best bets
5. "Junior" and "Senior" in colonial records indicate relative age in the community, not necessarily father and son
