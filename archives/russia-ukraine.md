# Russia and Ukraine

Genealogical research in Russia and Ukraine presents significant challenges: Cyrillic script, Soviet-era access restrictions, decentralized archives, border changes, and the destruction of records during WWII (particularly in Ukraine and Belarus). Despite these obstacles, FamilySearch has been digitizing records rapidly, JewishGen has indexed millions of records from the Pale of Settlement, and access is steadily improving.

## Key Databases

### FamilySearch (Russia/Ukraine collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Rapidly growing collection. Metric books (metricheskie knigi) from many guberniyas are being digitized. Includes Orthodox, Catholic, Lutheran, and Jewish records.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **Searchable**: Mostly browse-only (images organized by guberniya, uyezd, parish, and year). Some indexed collections.
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for images.

### JewishGen
- **URL**: https://www.jewishgen.org/
- **Coverage**: Essential for Jewish families from the Pale of Settlement (western Russia, Ukraine, Belarus, Poland, Lithuania). Town databases, vital records indexes, Yizkor books, Holocaust records, cemetery databases, and community histories.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by surname, town, record type)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. Structured results.

### VGD.ru (All-Russian Genealogical Forum)
- **URL**: https://forum.vgd.ru/
- **Coverage**: Russian genealogy forum with volunteer-indexed records, research assistance, and regional guides. The largest Russian-language genealogy community.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (forum search)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-readable (in Russian)

### Pamyat Naroda (Memory of the People)
- **URL**: https://pamyat-naroda.ru/
- **Coverage**: WWII Soviet military records. Unit histories, battle reports, award citations, personnel files.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, unit, date)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-readable (in Russian)

### OBD Memorial
- **URL**: https://obd-memorial.ru/
- **Coverage**: WWII casualties database. Killed in action, missing in action, prisoner of war records, burial records.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, rank, date, place)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable (in Russian)

### Geni.com
- **URL**: https://www.geni.com/
- **Coverage**: Significant Russian and Ukrainian family trees, often with sourced data. Useful for finding connections and existing research.
- **Cost**: Free (basic), subscription for full access
- **AI accessibility**: AI-readable

### Ukrainian State Archives (various)
- **Coverage**: The Central State Historical Archive of Ukraine in Kyiv (CDIAK) and Lviv (CDIAuL) hold the most extensive collections. Individual oblast archives hold records for their regions. Some have online catalogs; most require in-person or email research.
- **Cost**: Research fees may apply
- **AI accessibility**: Varies; most are in-person only

## Record Types

### Metric Books (Metricheskie Knigi)

The Russian Empire's equivalent of parish registers, maintained by all religious denominations under state supervision. Each metric book contains three sections:

1. **Births/Baptisms** (o rodivshikhsya): Name, date of birth and baptism, parents' names, godparents
2. **Marriages** (o brakosochetavshikhsya): Names and ages of bride and groom, witnesses
3. **Deaths** (ob umershikh): Name, age, cause of death, burial date

Metric books were kept by:
- **Orthodox churches**: Most records in Russian. The largest denomination. Covers the majority of the population.
- **Catholic churches**: Records in Latin or Polish. Common in western Ukraine, Belarus, and Lithuania.
- **Lutheran churches**: Records in German. Common in Baltic regions and German colonies.
- **Jewish communities**: Kept in separate books, often in Russian. JewishGen has extensive indexes.
- **Muslim communities**: Separate books. Less commonly digitized.

### Revision Lists (Revizskie Skazki)

The Russian Empire's censuses, conducted 10 times between 1719 and 1858. They list every member of a household with ages and are especially valuable because they cover serfs (who made up the vast majority of the population).

| Revision | Year | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | 1719 | Males only |
| 2nd | 1744 | Males only |
| 3rd | 1762 | Males only |
| 4th | 1782 | First to include females |
| 5th | 1795 | |
| 6th | 1811 | |
| 7th | 1816 | |
| 8th | 1834 | |
| 9th | 1850 | |
| 10th | 1858 | Last revision before emancipation (1861) |

Revision lists are the equivalent of the US census for pre-emancipation research: serfs were listed under their owner's estate. They provide a direct link between a serf family and a specific landowner, village, and estate. Available in state archives; some are being digitized on FamilySearch.

### Confiscated Church Records

After the 1917 Revolution, many church records were confiscated by the Soviet state and transferred to state archives. Ironically, this often preserved records that would otherwise have been lost. Civil registration (ZAGS, or Zapisi Aktov Grazhdanskogo Sostoyaniya) replaced church records as the official system.

## Administrative Divisions

The Russian Empire used a hierarchical system:
- **Guberniya** (province): The largest unit. Does not correspond to modern Russian oblasts or Ukrainian oblasts.
- **Uyezd** (county): Subdivision of a guberniya.
- **Volost** (township): Subdivision of a uyezd, the smallest administrative unit.

You need to know the pre-1917 guberniya and uyezd to locate records. Immigration records (ship manifests, naturalization papers) often list the guberniya or the town of origin. Maps of the Russian Empire's administrative divisions are essential.

### The Pale of Settlement

From 1791 to 1917, Jews in the Russian Empire were required to live within a defined area called the Pale of Settlement. It covered much of present-day Ukraine, Belarus, Lithuania, Latvia, Moldova, and eastern Poland. This geographic restriction means Jewish records are concentrated in specific guberniyas. JewishGen's databases are organized around these communities.

## Soviet-Era Complications

- After 1917, church records were confiscated and transferred to state archives (which actually preserved many of them).
- Civil registration (ZAGS) replaced church records for births, marriages, and deaths.
- Many records were destroyed during WWII, particularly in areas of heavy fighting across Ukraine, Belarus, and western Russia.
- Access to Soviet-era records can be complicated by privacy laws (records less than 75 years old may require proof of kinship) and bureaucratic procedures.
- Administrative boundaries were redrawn repeatedly during the Soviet period, making it difficult to trace which archive holds which records.

## Naming Conventions

Russian and Ukrainian names follow the pattern: **Given name + Patronymic + Surname**.

### Patronymics (Otchestvo)
The patronymic is derived from the father's given name:
- Male: Ivan Petrovich = Ivan, son of Pyotr
- Female: Maria Petrovna = Maria, daughter of Pyotr

Russian male patronymics end in **-ovich/-evich**. Female patronymics end in **-ovna/-evna**.

Ukrainian patronymics follow similar patterns but with different endings: **-ovich/-ivna** in Ukrainian vs **-ovich/-ovna** in Russian.

### Surname Patterns
- **-ov/-ev** (Russian), **-enko** (Ukrainian): Patronymic origin (Petrov = of Pyotr, Petrenko = of Pyotr)
- **-sky/-skiy**: Place of origin (Volkonsky = from the Volkon river)
- **-uk/-yuk** (Ukrainian): "Son of" (Petruk)
- **-ko** (Ukrainian): Diminutive or patronymic (Shevchenko)

### Jewish Naming in the Russian Empire
Jewish families in the Russian Empire were required to adopt fixed surnames in the early 1800s. Common patterns include place names (Berliner, Vilner), occupational names (Schneider, Reznik), and descriptive names (Klein, Schwartz). These were often in Yiddish or German, not Russian.

## Search Strategy

1. **Determine the guberniya and uyezd** from immigration records. Ship manifests and naturalization papers often list the town of origin or guberniya.
2. **Search FamilySearch** for digitized metric books from that area. Browse by guberniya, uyezd, and parish.
3. **For Jewish families**, search JewishGen town databases and vital records indexes. The JewishGen Locality Database identifies which records exist for each town.
4. **Check revision lists** for serf families (pre-1861). These link families to specific estates and villages.
5. **For WWII-era research**, search Pamyat Naroda (military service) and OBD Memorial (casualties).
6. **For modern records**, contact the relevant state archive (Russian or Ukrainian) or ZAGS office. Include the town name in both the current language and the pre-1917 Russian form.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Expecting to search by name.** Most records are browse-only images. You need to know the specific parish, town, or estate and then page through images year by year. Name-indexed databases (JewishGen, some FamilySearch collections) are the exception, not the rule.
- **Not knowing the pre-1917 administrative divisions.** Modern Russian oblasts and Ukrainian oblasts do not correspond to Imperial guberniyas. A town that was in Volhynia Guberniya in 1900 might be in Zhytomyr Oblast, Ukraine today. You need the old guberniya name to find records.
- **Assuming records were destroyed in WWII.** Many records survive in state archives, especially those transferred from churches during the Soviet period. The destruction was real but uneven. Always check before giving up.
- **Language barrier.** Records are in Cyrillic (Russian or Church Slavonic), Latin (Catholic), German (Lutheran), or Polish. Learning to read Cyrillic handwriting, or at least the key genealogical terms, is essential.
- **Confusing Russian and Ukrainian administrative systems.** After 1991, Ukrainian archives reorganized under a different system. A record that was in a "Russian" archive before independence is now in a Ukrainian state archive, but finding aids may still reference the old system.
- **The Pale of Settlement boundary.** For Jewish research, knowing whether a town was inside or outside the Pale determines which databases and record types are relevant. Towns just outside the Pale may have very few Jewish records.
- **Ignoring the revision lists.** For serf families (the vast majority of the population before 1861), revision lists are often the only source that names individual family members. They are underused relative to their value.
