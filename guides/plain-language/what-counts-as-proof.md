# What Counts As Proof

Proof in genealogy means the evidence is strong enough to rely on, not that every possible doubt is gone.

## A Useful Standard

A claim is usually ready to add when:

- You opened the source yourself.
- The source supports the exact claim.
- The identity match makes sense.
- The source is strong enough for the claim.
- No better source contradicts it.
- Uncertainty is recorded honestly.

## What Is Usually Enough

Fictional examples:

- A birth certificate names the child and parents.
- A marriage record names both spouses and matches the known location.
- A probate file names children and connects them to known family members.
- Two independent records agree on a relationship and no serious conflict exists.

## What Is Usually Not Enough

- One unsourced family tree.
- One same-name search result.
- A cemetery memorial with no linked relatives or photo.
- A DNA ethnicity estimate.
- A family story with no record support.

## Proof Can Be Narrow

One source may prove one fact but not another.

Fictional example:

A death certificate may prove that Rowan Vale died on a specific date. It may be weaker evidence for Rowan's birth date if the informant did not know it directly.

## When To Stop

You can accept a claim when the evidence is strong enough for your purpose and the remaining uncertainty is written down.

You should keep researching when:

- The claim changes parentage.
- The claim adds a new ancestral line.
- Sources conflict.
- The only support is copied or vague.
- A living person's privacy is involved.
