# African American Genealogy

African American genealogy requires a fundamentally different approach than standard American genealogy. Before the 1870 census, enslaved people were recorded in slave schedules by age, sex, and color only, rarely by name. The 1870 census is the first to list formerly enslaved people by name. This creates a "brick wall" at 1870 for most African American researchers. Breaking through requires different sources, different strategies, and a willingness to research the slaveholder's family in order to find records about your own.

## Key Databases

### Freedmen's Bureau Records
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/search/collection/1417695
- **Coverage**: 3.5 million records (1865 to 1872) including labor contracts, marriage records, hospital records, school records, ration lists, and complaints. The single most important post-emancipation source.
- **Cost**: Free (on FamilySearch)
- **Searchable**: Partially indexed. Many records are browse-only images.
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for images. Some indexed portions are searchable.

### Freedman's Bank Records
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/search/collection/1417695
- **Coverage**: Applications for bank accounts (1865 to 1874) that include birthplace, age, complexion, occupation, spouse, children, siblings, parents, and sometimes the former slaveholder's name. Extraordinarily detailed.
- **Cost**: Free (on FamilySearch)
- **Searchable**: Yes (indexed and searchable by name)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable for the index; original images require manual review

### US Federal Census Slave Schedules (1850, 1860)
- **Coverage**: List enslaved people by slaveholder name, county, age, sex, and color. Names are rarely included; people are identified only by demographic information.
- **Cost**: Free on FamilySearch; also on Ancestry
- **Searchable**: Searchable by slaveholder name. Not searchable by enslaved person's name (because names were not recorded).
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (images)

### Ancestry.com
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.com/
- **Coverage**: Largest commercial collection of African American records including Freedmen's Bureau, slave narratives (WPA Federal Writers' Project), Freedman's Bank, and USCT military records. Some African American and slavery collections are permanently free.
- **Cost**: Subscription (some collections free)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required for most)

### FamilySearch
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Extensive free collections including Freedmen's Bureau, Freedman's Bank, census records, and state-level records
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for records

### Enslaved.org
- **URL**: https://enslaved.org/
- **Coverage**: Aggregated database of records of enslaved people drawn from multiple institutions and research projects. Links records across collections.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, location, date)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

### Last Seen: Finding Family After Slavery
- **URL**: https://informationwanted.org/
- **Coverage**: Database of "information wanted" advertisements placed by formerly enslaved people in newspapers after the Civil War. These ads sought lost family members separated by sale or war and contain names, relationships, locations, and slaveholder names.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (by name, location)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable

### Afro-American Historical and Genealogical Society (AAHGS)
- **URL**: https://aahgs.org/
- **Coverage**: Publications, research guides, and regional chapters focused on African American genealogy
- **Cost**: Membership for full access
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-readable

### National Museum of African American History and Culture (NMAAHC)
- **Coverage**: Robert Frederick Smith Family History Center in Washington, DC. Free, in-person genealogy assistance with professional genealogists and access to subscription databases.
- **Cost**: Free (in-person only)
- **AI accessibility**: Not applicable (in-person resource)

## The 1870 Brick Wall: Pre-Emancipation Research Strategies

The core challenge of African American genealogy is connecting a named individual in the 1870 census to their identity before emancipation, when they were recorded without a name. The following strategies work in combination to break through this wall.

### 1. Start with the 1870 Census

Find your ancestor in the 1870 census. Record the county, township, neighbors, age, and birthplace. The 1870 census is the anchor point; everything before it requires working backward from this record.

Note who lives nearby. In many cases, formerly enslaved people settled near their former slaveholder's property. The slaveholder may appear on the same page or within a few pages. White neighbors with the same surname as your ancestor are strong leads.

### 2. Freedmen's Bureau Records (1865 to 1872)

Marriage records filed with the Bureau often state where the couple was enslaved and by whom. Labor contracts name both the freedperson and the former slaveholder (and sometimes include family members). Complaints and correspondence can reveal family relationships, former plantations, and geographic movement.

These records are organized by state and subdistrict. They are partially indexed on FamilySearch and Ancestry, but many records have not been indexed and require page-by-page browsing.

### 3. Identify the Slaveholder

Once you have a likely slaveholder candidate (from the 1870 census proximity, Freedmen's Bureau records, or surname analysis), research the slaveholder's family. This is counterintuitive but essential: the slaveholder's paper trail often holds the key to your ancestor's identity.

Key slaveholder records:
- **Wills and estate inventories**: Enslaved people are sometimes named in wills, especially when bequeathed to specific heirs. Estate inventories list every person by name, age, and sometimes family grouping.
- **Tax records**: County tax records list slaveholders and the number of enslaved people they held. Useful for tracking a slaveholder across years.
- **Slave schedules (1850, 1860)**: Match your ancestor's age and sex against the slaveholder's slave schedule. If you know a family group (parents and children), matching the entire group's ages increases confidence.
- **Deeds of sale**: County deed books record sales of enslaved people, sometimes by name.
- **Plantation records**: Birth, death, and family registers kept by some plantation owners. These are in university special collections, historical societies, and some on FamilySearch.

### 4. Church Records

Some churches recorded baptisms and marriages of enslaved people, sometimes by name. Quaker meeting records are particularly good for this because Quakers documented enslaved people more carefully than most denominations. Catholic parish records in Louisiana are another strong source.

### 5. Surname Analysis

Many formerly enslaved people took the surname of their last slaveholder, but not always. Some took the name of an earlier slaveholder (one the family considered more significant), a name of their own choosing (sometimes a prominent figure like Washington or Jefferson), or a name reflecting their trade or origin. Compare your ancestor's surname against slaveholders in the same county, but do not assume a match means a direct connection.

### 6. DNA Testing

For African American genealogy, DNA is not supplementary; it is often a primary research tool. The paper trail before 1870 is thin by design (enslavers had no incentive to document enslaved families). DNA can:

- Connect you to relatives whose paper trail survived where yours did not
- Identify slaveholder families through shared DNA with their descendants
- Confirm or rule out suspected family connections
- Reveal African ethnic origins that no paper record can provide

AncestryDNA has the largest matching database. 23andMe and FamilyTreeDNA are also useful. Uploading results to GEDmatch expands your match pool further.

## Post-1870 Sources

After 1870, standard US genealogy sources apply (see usa-census.md, usa-vital-records.md), but additional sources specific to African American history are also available:

### United States Colored Troops (USCT) Records
Approximately 180,000 African American men served in the Union Army during the Civil War. Military service records, pension files, and compiled military service records are held at the National Archives. Pension files are particularly valuable because they contain depositions from family members and fellow soldiers that describe pre-war life, family relationships, and sometimes slavery-era details.

### Southern Claims Commission (1871 to 1880)
Formerly enslaved people who aided the Union during the Civil War could file claims for property taken by Union forces. Claims include depositions with detailed biographical information, descriptions of wartime activities, and testimony from neighbors.

### Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCU) Archives
Many HBCUs maintain archives with records of students, faculty, and community members from the late 1800s onward.

### Black Newspapers
African American newspapers published social news, obituaries, church activities, and family information not found in white-owned newspapers. Accessible through Accessible Archives, ProQuest Historical Newspapers, and Newspapers.com.

### Freedman's Bank Records (1865 to 1874)
Bank account applications are among the most detailed records available for this period. A single application can list an individual's birthplace, age, complexion, occupation, spouse, children, siblings, parents (including maiden name of mother), and sometimes the name of the former slaveholder.

## Naming Conventions

Post-emancipation naming was complex and does not follow a single pattern.

### Surnames
Surnames adopted after 1865 may reflect:
- The last slaveholder's name (most common, but not universal)
- An earlier slaveholder's name (sometimes from a slaveholder the family was with for a longer period or considered more significant)
- A chosen name unrelated to any slaveholder (often a prominent figure, a trade, or a geographic feature)
- A name used within the enslaved community that differed from what appeared in slaveholder records

### First Names
- Some first names have African origins that were replaced with English names in slaveholder records
- Naming after relatives was common, similar to other American naming patterns
- Unusual or distinctive first names can be valuable for tracking individuals across records

### Name Changes Over Time
An individual might appear under different names in different records: a slaveholder-given name, a name used within the enslaved community, and a legal name adopted after emancipation. Freedmen's Bureau records sometimes document these transitions explicitly.

## Search Strategy

1. **1870 census**: Locate your ancestor by name. Record the county, neighbors, ages, and birthplace. This is the foundation.
2. **Freedmen's Bureau records**: Search for labor contracts, marriage records, and other documents that name both your ancestor and their former slaveholder.
3. **Freedman's Bank records**: Search for account applications that list family members and biographical details.
4. **Identify the likely slaveholder** from census proximity, Bureau records, or surname analysis.
5. **Research the slaveholder's records**: Wills, estate inventories, tax records, deeds of sale, and slave schedules.
6. **Slave schedules (1850, 1860)**: Match ages and family groupings against the identified slaveholder's schedule.
7. **Plantation records**: Search university special collections and FamilySearch for records from the identified plantation.
8. **DNA matching**: Test with AncestryDNA (largest database) and upload to GEDmatch. Look for matches among descendants of the suspected slaveholder family and among other African American researchers.
9. **Church records**: Check denominations active in the area, especially Quaker and Catholic records.
10. **USCT and pension records**: If your ancestor or a family member served in the Civil War, pension files can contain extensive family testimony.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming the surname matches the last slaveholder.** It often does, but many families chose a different name. Do not eliminate a slaveholder candidate solely because the surnames do not match.
- **Not searching Freedmen's Bureau records.** These records are underused by researchers. They are the bridge between enslavement and freedom, and they often contain the slaveholder's name, family relationships, and geographic details that no other source provides.
- **Ignoring DNA as a research tool.** For African American genealogy before 1870, DNA is often the most productive avenue when paper records are exhausted. It is not a last resort; it should be used early and in parallel with documentary research.
- **Giving up at 1870.** Many records exist before 1870; they just require different strategies. Slaveholder wills, estate inventories, plantation records, church records, and deeds of sale can identify enslaved people by name decades before emancipation.
- **Not researching the slaveholder family.** This feels counterintuitive and can be emotionally difficult, but the slaveholder's paper trail is where your ancestor's name is most likely to appear in pre-1870 records. Wills and estate divisions are the most productive slaveholder records.
- **Searching only by the ancestor's name before 1870.** Enslaved people were rarely recorded by name in public records. Pre-1870 research requires searching by the slaveholder's name and then identifying your ancestor within those records by matching age, sex, and family groupings.
- **Overlooking "information wanted" ads.** The Last Seen project (https://informationwanted.org/) contains thousands of newspaper advertisements placed by formerly enslaved people searching for family members. These ads are rich in detail and are fully searchable.
- **Not considering multiple slaveholders.** Enslaved people were frequently sold, inherited, or hired out to different slaveholders. Your ancestor may appear in the records of several different slaveholder families across their lifetime.
