# USA: Vital Records

Vital records (birth, marriage, death certificates) are primary sources and the gold standard for genealogical evidence. Access varies significantly by state and era.

## Overview

Vital record registration in the US is a state-by-state system. There is no national vital records database.

### When Registration Began

| Era | States | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1841 to 1860 | Massachusetts, New Jersey, and a few others | Earliest state-level registration |
| 1860 to 1900 | Most northeastern and midwestern states | Coverage grows but compliance is spotty |
| 1900 to 1920 | Most remaining states | Federal Birth Registration Area established 1915 |
| 1920 to 1933 | All states | Texas was last to join the Birth Registration Area (1933) |

**Before state registration**: Check county records (county clerk, recorder of deeds) and church records.

## Access

### State Vital Records Offices
Every state has a vital records office. Most have online ordering for certified copies.
- **Cost**: $10 to $30 per certificate (varies by state)
- **Turnaround**: 2 to 12 weeks
- **AI accessibility**: In-person or mail order only for certified copies

### FamilySearch
- **Coverage**: Many states have digitized older vital records (typically records older than 75 to 100 years)
- **Cost**: Free
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for individual records

### Ancestry.com
- **Coverage**: Large collections of indexed vital records
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only

### County Clerks
For records predating state registration, the county clerk or recorder of deeds is often the best source.
- **Cost**: Varies ($5 to $25 per record)
- **AI accessibility**: In-person, phone, or mail request

## Record Types

### Birth Certificates
- **What they contain**: Full name, date and place of birth, parents' names (including mother's maiden name), parents' ages, parents' birthplaces, father's occupation, attendant at birth
- **Delayed certificates**: Filed years or decades after the event. Common for rural births and births before mandatory registration. The delay introduces potential errors (informant's memory).
- **Research tip**: Mother's maiden name on a birth certificate is one of the most reliable genealogical facts. It often resolves maiden name disputes.

### Marriage Records
- **What they contain**: Names of both parties, ages, places of birth, parents' names, date and place of marriage, officiant, witnesses
- **License vs certificate**: The marriage license is filed before the wedding; the marriage certificate or return is filed after. Both may contain slightly different information.
- **Research tip**: Witnesses are often family members. Note their names and research them separately.

### Death Certificates
- **What they contain**: Full name, date and place of death, cause of death, date and place of birth, parents' names, occupation, marital status, spouse's name, informant's name
- **Research tip**: The informant matters. If the informant was the deceased's child, they may not know the deceased's parents' full names or exact birth date. This explains why death certificates sometimes have errors in the "parents' names" field.

## State-Specific Notes

Different states have different public access rules for vital records:

| Access Level | Example States | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Open access (older records) | Many states | Records older than 75 to 100 years are public |
| Restricted (next of kin only) | California, New York | Only close relatives can order; varies by record type |
| Very restricted | Some states | Death records may be open while birth records are restricted |

## Search Strategy

1. Determine the state and approximate date of the vital event
2. Check FamilySearch for digitized older records
3. If not on FamilySearch, check whether the state vital records office or county clerk has online ordering
4. For pre-state-registration records, contact the county clerk directly
5. For marriage records, check both the county where the marriage occurred and the county where the license was issued (sometimes different)
6. Cross-reference vital record data with census records and church records to verify
