# Norway

Norwegian genealogical records are among the best-digitized and most accessible in the world. Most are free.

## Key Databases

### Digitalarkivet (Digital Archives of Norway)
- **URL**: https://www.digitalarkivet.no/en/search/persons
- **Coverage**: Census records (1801, 1865, 1875, 1891, 1900, 1910), church records, emigration protocols, military rolls
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Yes (person search by name, parish, time period)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable. The person search returns structured results that AI tools can read.
- **Languages**: Norwegian and English interface available

**Key collections:**
- **Census records**: 1801 is the earliest complete census. 1865 onward includes occupation, birthplace, and religion.
- **Emigration records**: Police emigration protocols from major ports (Ålesund 1879 to 1925, Bergen 1874 to 1930, Oslo/Christiania 1867 to 1930). These are the single most information-dense Norwegian genealogy source for immigrant families.
- **Church records (kirkebøker)**: Baptisms, confirmations, marriages, burials. Most parishes covered from the 1700s onward. These are browse-only (scanned images), not name-searchable.

**Search tips:**
- Search by farm name, not surname, for pre-1900 records
- Try both modern and historical spellings of place names
- Patronymic names change by generation ("Rasmusen" is only used by Rasmus's sons, not later descendants)

### National Library of Norway (nb.no)
- **URL**: https://www.nb.no/
- **Coverage**: Digitized books including bygdebøker (local history books), newspapers, maps
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Full-text search on OCR'd books
- **AI accessibility**: AI-readable. Search results and book pages can be fetched.

**Key resource**: Bygdebøker (farm and parish histories) often contain multi-generational farm lineage data going back to the 1600s. Search for your parish name + "bygdebok" or "gard og slekt."

### FamilySearch (Norway collections)
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: Indexed church records, census records, emigration records
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only for individual records (login required). Wiki pages are AI-readable.

### Kvien Database
- **URL**: https://kvien.net/
- **Coverage**: Western Norway farm lineages, particularly Nordfjord area
- **Cost**: Free
- **AI accessibility**: AI-readable (simple HTML pages)
- **Note**: Volunteer-maintained. Excellent for specific farms in Sogn og Fjordane.

## Naming Conventions

Norwegian genealogy requires understanding three naming systems:

1. **Patronymics**: Before ~1900, children took their father's first name as a surname suffix. Rasmus's son becomes "[Name] Rasmusen." Rasmus's daughter becomes "[Name] Rasmusdatter." This means a family's "surname" changes every generation.

2. **Farm names**: People were identified by the farm where they lived. Moving to a new farm meant taking a new name. "Ole Nordli" means "Ole who lives at the Nordli farm."

3. **Fixed surnames**: After ~1900 (law of 1923), Norwegians adopted fixed hereditary surnames. Many chose their farm name. Immigrants often adopted their patronymic or farm name as a fixed surname earlier.

## Search Strategy

1. Start with Digitalarkivet person search for your known ancestor
2. If found in census, note the farm name and parish
3. Search the same farm in earlier censuses
4. Search nb.no for a bygdebok covering that parish
5. Search FamilySearch wiki for the parish to find available church records
6. Check emigration records for departure details

## Common Pitfalls

- "Nordli" may not appear in the Oluf Rygh Farm Name Index if it is a husmannsplass (sub-farm)
- The same person may appear under different names in different records
- Geographic boundaries have changed: Sogn og Fjordane and Hordaland merged into Vestland county in 2020
- Some church records were lost in fires or never created for remote parishes
